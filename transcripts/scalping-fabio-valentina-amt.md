## Why 90% of Traders Lose — The Market Structure Misconception

**Fabio:** The concept of market structure that most traders follow involves watching for a market structure shift, change of character, or breakout — there are a hundred terms for it. They try to go trend following when a pattern like this presents, waiting for a retracement to jump in — using supply or order blocks, mitigation levels — and continue in the direction of the trend.

What I've tested over many years of experience is that this pattern usually doesn't work if you don't frame it in the correct concept. To frame it correctly, you need to start using something necessary: **the profile**. You need to understand exactly where you are in the market and what the big picture is.

> **Key Insight:** If a trend-following pattern presents when the bell curve of the volume distribution looks like this, you are jumping on a trend that goes against the lower distribution of volume. Statistically, only 30% of these breakouts will follow through. The rest will remain in what's called the fair value area. You will keep getting stopped out until the market decides to take off.

This is the reason many traders who try trend-following strategies find their win rate is not what they expected — 70%, 80% — when they try to target a level. They think they'll have 50–60% win rate at 1:3 risk-to-reward, but that's not real. You are not reading what the market is saying; you are trying to predict the market.

## Auction Market Theory — The Foundation

**Fabio:** The good point about order flow and volume analysis in general is that you get the knowledge to understand when to stay away. If the same trend-following pattern presents in the right area, it's a completely different situation. To explain this, we need to go to **AMT — Auction Market Theory**.

AMT is the real nature of the market. The market is an auction that goes from **balanced areas** — where all market participants are transacting efficiently — to **imbalance**. The market is not usually in imbalance. It's usually in balance.

> **Key Insight:** This is why traders take streaks of stop-losses — they try to catch the last spring before the move up and notice that their 1:10 risk-to-reward becomes 1:2 because they take four stops trying to get it right.

If you make one simple change — waiting for the market to get to a condition of **out of balance** — your win rate will jump by at least 20–30%. This is exactly the building point of the model that I use.

> **Key Insight:** I call it a "model" and not a "strategy" because a strategy is a group of rules you follow strictly. But how can you follow rules strictly without understanding the narrative if the market is a dynamic entity? It's like trying to cage an animal that is not made to be in a cage. The market changes every day and adapts.

## The Three-Step Model Framework

The model has three sequential steps that must all be present before executing a trade.

### Step 1: Market State — Location (Balance vs. Imbalance)

You use the profile to understand where you are. The model is only valid to trade when the market is **out of balance**. This is your filter that keeps you out of the market when conditions aren't right.

When the market is out of balance, you start seeing what all traders are using — market structure — and validate which areas can be used to jump on a trade. This is where supply and demand zones and inefficiency become useful.

> **Key Insight:** Inefficiency is misconceived because people use the term "fair value gap." What really happens behind the curtains is when one side is more aggressive than the other — the market gets momentum in one direction. This is the best moment to transact because the market is telling you: "I'm out of balance. I'm searching for a new level of balance."

### Step 2: Level Validation — Using Order Flow and Low Volume Nodes

You have the direction, the condition of the market, and three possible take-profit areas. Now order flow becomes useful — it's not just a concept but an entirely new alphabet for reading the market.

90% of traders try to understand what's happening inside a candle using multi-timeframe analysis. The real inefficiency is how traders analyze the market — it's like trying to hit a bullseye blinded. You can use the area that creates breakouts and zoom in using profile to watch exactly where there are **low volume nodes**.

> **Key Insight:** A low volume node is a really good reaction level. If you have a low volume node here, the probability that price will continue through it is really high. You start adding the concept of refinement — you have the direction, the location, and the refinement of the location.

### Step 3: Execution Trigger — Aggression Detection

This is the part that requires experience and cannot be fully automated. You analyze **big orders** — when there is, in one specific point, a lot of aggression. On an order flow platform, you typically see bubbles representing large orders.

> **Key Insight:** When there is direction, location, and aggression — your ability to predict is zero, but your ability to read is 100%. You are tuning into the market at the correct moment. You are not predicting what it will do. You are waiting.

When you see aggression, you don't need a huge stop loss out of balance. You can get protected exactly above the big sell aggression. Your risk-to-reward is really big, and the probability of your trades is also really big. You are swimming in the direction of the flow — being pulled down by market aggression and by all the traders who were long and will close their position when the area breaks.

The target point is based on the **previous balance area**, because the market seeks balance. If the previous point of control — where the majority of volume was located — is here, the probability of the market reaching it is high.

## Model 1: Trend Following (New York Session)

### Timing

New York session only. We want the highest volatility for this model. All positions are closed at market close — no overnight trades, because you need more margin for futures and it's not convenient.

### Step 1: Market State — Imbalance

We can only have two market states: **balanced** and **imbalanced**. With this model, we only want to transact during imbalance. We are not interested in balanced market conditions.

> **Key Insight:** This is the lock that prevents you from entering unless the market tells you exactly what it needs to. Every small impulse is not a setup — taking three stops for the day and then missing the big move is the cost of ignoring market state.

### Step 2: Location — Swing Points and Low Volume Nodes

The location is your swing point. You need to analyze it deeply to understand whether to jump in here, there, or even from the lowest point if momentum is high enough that you can cover yourself with a tight stop.

You use **volume profile** and search for **low volume nodes** — the point where the lowest volume was transacted. I don't place limit orders at these levels. I set an alert slightly below, because when price arrives there, I want to see big trades — big orders — before committing.

### Step 3: Execution — Big Red Bubbles

What you want to see is **aggression**. If you are selling, you want a big red bubble. When you see it, you jump in. Your stop loss goes above the aggression, and your target is the previous balance area.

We take out the **full position** at the target because the probability of reversal is 70%. It's not worth holding for only 30% probability of continuation.

| Component | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Session** | New York |
| **Market State** | Imbalance (out of balance) |
| **Location** | Swing points, low volume nodes |
| **Trigger** | Large aggressive orders (big red/green bubbles) |
| **Target** | Previous balance area / point of control |
| **Exit** | Full position at target (70% reversal probability) |
| **Stop Loss** | Above the aggression candle |

## Model 2: Mean Reversion (London Session / Consolidation)

### Market State — Consolidation

Model 2 uses the opposite market state: **consolidation**. This is when the profile is protecting price from breaking the range boundaries — bouncing from high to low and back.

What we are trying to take is the out-of-balance condition that **snaps back inside balance**. We do not take the first swing because it's risky. We wait for the **second swing** — when the first breakout happens, we wait for the retracement.

### Location and Execution

The location is the swing. You frame the location, wait for the retracement, look for a low volume node, and watch for seller aggression. If the target is the point of control — where the bulk of auctions are taking place — that's where you aim.

> **Key Insight:** The error everyone makes is targeting the extreme (the high or the low). The probability is not high. You target where the bulk of volume transacted — where the probability of returning to balance is really high.

If you are wrong, you want to be wrong immediately. If you see big sell orders, your stop loss goes right there — one or two ticks below the high, not above it.

### Slippage Protection Trick

> **Key Insight:** Place your stop loss one or two ticks below the high instead of above it. Above the high there are many orders, and the market will accelerate through — you lose additional ticks. By getting taken out before the acceleration, you save five or six ticks per trade. Statistically, it's worth it over the long term.

| Component | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Session** | London (best for summer months / consolidation periods) |
| **Market State** | Consolidation / balance |
| **Location** | Swing extremes, low volume nodes |
| **Trigger** | Second swing with aggression confirmation |
| **Target** | Point of control / high-volume area |
| **Exit** | Full position at target |
| **Stop Loss** | One or two ticks below the extreme |

### Using Both Models Together

There are blessed days where you get Model 2 during London session, then the market breaks the range, and you activate Model 1 for New York — taking two amazing targets and being done for the week. When I lose with one model, I profit with the other. It balances the equity curve, giving more execution while lowering drawdown.

## Why Order Flow Over Price Action

**Fabio:** I was a price action trader, but I'm really open-minded. If someone can improve my risk-to-reward using a new tool, I'll use it. A trader needs to be the most open-minded person because if new data — like MBO orders — can improve your model, you shouldn't let your ego block it.

I went to order flow also because price action is harder to replicate for students. If 100 people watch this and we say "use a break of structure in favor of the trend" — for one person a break of structure is this, for another it's an external break, for another they take the big swing. But if I say "the profile is this — this is the level where you are out of balance" — it cannot be this, it cannot be that. It's objective.

> **Key Insight:** Three levels of analysis exist: **lagging** (indicators like MACD, RSI, Stochastic — derivatives of price that always follow), **real-time** (price action — watching what's happening as an effect), and **leading** (volume — watching the narrative before price creates it).

With price action, you can only put a trade to break even when price breaks a level. With volume, you can use **CVD (Cumulative Volume Delta)** — a benchmark for pressure of volume — to see aggressive buyers pushing on the gas before price confirms. This lets you move to break even sooner than other people.

### The Auction Analogy

The market works like a real auction. If there's a limited 1969 Mustang with a base auction of $1 million and buyers start bidding $1.2M, $1.4M, $1.6M — the price is probably going up because they are interested. At the market price of $2.1M, participants start bidding in smaller increments ($50K not $200K), the market gets lower, and you build new balance. The point of control is where the auctioneer closes — the level of highest volume.

## Edge Decay and the Advantage of Order Flow

**Fabio:** Edge decay is when your model loses its edge. I noticed this a lot with price action. For example, when I was using supply and demand in trend following and volatility changed — the length of swings changed — I would take a lot of stop-losses not because the direction was wrong, but because the market swung wider.

> **Key Insight:** With order flow, you cannot have edge decay because what you're watching is the true nature of the market. You are not framing the market in closed rules from A to B — you are understanding what's happening.

A simple exercise improved my model by 4–5% in win rate (which is enormous for a scalper): When you see a retracement in a range, instead of just trading the retest with price action, zoom into order flow on that same candle. Watch cumulative volume delta and orders. If you see heavy aggression at a level and CVD is diverging, the movement is not supported, and the probability of reversal is high — and this information comes before price stops you out. You can decide not to jump in.

## Cumulative Volume Delta — Leading Indicator for Break-Even Timing

**Fabio:** CVD gives you a benchmark for volume pressure. When you are out of balance and the probability of finding new balance is high, CVD shows this before price does.

Imagine price is moving up and CVD is pushing up aggressively — buyers are willing to buy at higher prices, and sellers are not aggressive. If you're at an auction and buyers are bidding higher increments, the price is going up. When you see CVD pushing up and the leg is already building, you can already move to break even. As the smallest retracement happens, aggressive buyers will continue pushing — and you are protected.

> **Key Insight:** CVD lets you move to break even before price action traders can. It's a leading indicator — you're watching the narrative before price creates the effect.

## Pre-Market Preparation and Narrative Building

**Fabio:** In pre-market, I mark the biggest delta volume — both buy and sell sides with different colors. I want to know which levels get tapped at market opening. From the buy side, I take the swing low and identify the level with the most volume — the cluster of buy volume. From the sell side, same process.

These are the levels I expect to see reactions at within the first 15–20 minutes. I work on the five-minute chart for analysis, then drop to one minute for execution.

> **Key Insight:** Never trade 10–20 minutes before the session opens. The volatility range changes by session. A model that works for London is not guaranteed to generate alpha in New York. You will be liquidated, and even if your direction is right, volatility will destroy your position.

### Pre-Market Narrative Rules

Before the market opens, build a narrative based on the data. If sellers are aggressive at a level but getting absorbed by buyers with no follow-through, your long narrative holds. If price breaks a balance level to the downside, your narrative shifts to short.

> **Key Insight:** Don't marry your position. Be ready to change your narrative the moment the data tells you to. Your ego will try to keep you in a bias — resist it. The market doesn't care about your opinion.

## Managing the Trade — Scale-Out, Squeeze, and Trailing Stops

**Fabio:** When I told you I would scale out, it's exactly because when you see aggressive sellers step in, the next step is either their recovery (squeeze) or their follow-through (reversal). You coded the algorithm — the next step is aggressive sellers.

If aggressive sellers get recovered, we should expect another explosion up. If not, we protect our profit.

### Trailing Stop Philosophy

I will not move to break even too fast because I know NASDAQ. At the session open, it typically gives the first drive, pulls back, then creates the real move. If I go risk-free too quickly, the market can spike me out at the low volume node and then rocket up — and I'm out of my position.

Wait for a full body candle accepting the high before moving to break even. When you see candle acceptance, you can trail aggressively.

### The Squeeze Mechanics

All the red seller orders at the top are underwater. When they need to close their position, the market jumps — and when it reaches the level where aggressive sellers got trapped, you see the acceleration. This is the same pattern in London but three times larger in New York.

> **Key Insight:** The squeeze happens when trapped sellers are forced to close. Each closed sell position creates more upward momentum. This is why understanding order flow is so powerful — you can see the pain building before the price move happens.

## Risk Management and Position Sizing

**Fabio:** I risk 0.25% per trade on personal accounts. In the Robins Cup, I went up to 0.5% per trade, and sometimes 1% after reaching 100% returns. My rule is always keeping drawdown low because commission is heavy — 600 executions per quarter can cost 10–20% of the account.

> **Key Insight:** You don't only need to make profit — you need to repay commission. It's not convenient to over-execute.

The strategy is about 33% of the equation. Another 33% is the **mindset** — the ability to stay calm when your setup isn't presenting. The final 33% is **risk management**.

| Risk Parameter | Value |
|----------------|-------|
| **Per-trade risk (personal)** | 0.25% |
| **Per-trade risk (competition)** | 0.5–1% |
| **Max daily loss** | 2% (equates to ~8 stop-losses) |
| **Target R:R minimum** | 1:2.5 |
| **Target R:R common** | 1:5 |
| **Max daily stop-losses before stopping** | 8 (benchmark: 11 in worst case) |

### Building Position Size Over Time

Start with micros. Build over time. If it takes 2–4 years to build position size, confidence, and skill — that's worth being able to make $4,000–$10,000 in a day. Don't hear that number and think you can do it immediately. It's the 3–5 years of dedication that allows you to have that skill set.

### The Compound Strategy

Build profit for the day, build profit for the day, build profit for the day. On directional days, risk the profit you made across 10 trades. If the day closes, that profit is locked in as equity — you cannot risk it. The next day resets.

> **Key Insight:** This is how I achieved performance in the world trading cup — accumulating daily profits, then on the best days, using those profits to take aggressive shots with no downside to the account.

## Consolidation Days and When to Stop Trading

**Fabio:** The model doesn't work properly during compression. In the worst market environment, we still managed to make $1,000 profit because of risk management. We took profit when necessary, and when we were wrong, we were wrong immediately.

On a day like this — gap down, recovery, compression, fake breakouts — the only trending environment was the initial move, and we monetized it. Everything else was managing risk and surviving.

> **Key Insight:** The goal of every trader is to make money or close at break-even on the days your model isn't working. If you have a mean-reverting day and you close break-even or small profit, you're great. Same for trend-following days. Wait for the explosion days — that's where the real money is made.

### When to Stop

If you take a stop loss on the second model attempt and the market is in full consolidation, stop for the day. Protect the profit you made. Tomorrow is another day, and you wait for the explosion.

Monday and Friday are typically the worst-performing days because they are not explosion days — the profit factor is much lower. Still profitable, but lower quality.
