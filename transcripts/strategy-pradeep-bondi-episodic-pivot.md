## The Single Factor That Determines Trading Success

**Pradeep:** The one single factor which determines whether somebody makes it or not in this business is basically their **self-leadership**. The people who have this ability to find solutions to their trading problem — because trading is not easy. You have to find solutions to a lot of problems in order to make it work. Even if there is a template somebody gives you saying this is the setup and this is how you should do it, you have to make it work for yourself.

Every one of the highly successful traders I have seen has this innate ability, this self-leadership, where they can guide their own performance, they can correct their mistakes, and they can keep themselves motivated — because there are periods when you don't become profitable in this business. It takes two, three years to put all these things together, and they have that self-efficacy belief, that fundamental belief, that no matter what, I'm going to make this work. And they don't give up because of that particular belief.

**Host:** Do you think a part of that also is the mastery of the trading process and actually a passion for the trading process and the markets as a whole versus just being focused on the monetary aspect?

**Pradeep:** I think it's a combination. I know a lot of people who are very passionate about the market, very passionate about trading, but they are not necessarily making money. So it's not necessarily that passion is going to make you money.

What I see when I talk to traders is there is something called **mind clarity** which you need to have about how traders make money. A lot of time people come to me and say, "I don't know any day trader who's making money." Now, the people who are outsiders or who are new to trading, they don't know this. They have this gap between what actual traders do and what they *think* traders do. Once that mind clarity comes in and you figure out how this game is actually played, that's when things change. Because a lot of time, books on trading are very misleading. If you read a book on trading by, say, a Market Wizard, he shows two or three best examples of a trade. But there are a lot of things which don't work.

## Singles vs. Home Runs — How Profitable Traders Actually Make Money

A lot of money in trading is made by people by **hitting singles**, while all the books are about home runs. If you talk to successful traders — most of the people featured on your podcast — they had occasional home runs, but there are thousands of singles. Those singles are the game. But for a new person coming in, he thinks, "I come into this to buy an island. I don't want to do this hitting singles."

> **Key Insight:** The majority of your P&L will probably be made up of those home run trades — there will only be a very small handful a year, but they may make up 70–80% of your P&L. However, you still need those base hits.

Without those base hits, you won't have the mental clarity and the viewpoint on the market. You won't be sharp with what's going on. But most of all, to make that huge P&L and to be able to size more, you need those base hit profits.

**Pradeep:** Your singles allow you to finance your larger trades. Because if you're dependent on a larger trade and it doesn't work out, and you have a 10% drawdown, 15% drawdown, now you are under pressure — "I need to find another home run." But if you have a combination strategy of singles and home runs, then you are not under pressure. You can say, "Okay, this particular trade didn't work out, but I can hit 25 singles, 30 singles and make up for it."

Second, in terms of sheer learning, it's much easier to learn something which you can do with higher frequency. If you're taking a trade you're going to hold for 6 months or 1 year, you're going to get feedback about whether it worked only 6 months or a year down the line. Your learning is going to be very slow. When you do day trading or singles as a strategy — I do two-to-three-day hold swings, which is my equivalent of singles — the feedback loop is immediately there, and then you can correct.

For a learner, you need to try something which is higher frequency first. Then you might transition to longer-term holds. But if I had to learn, I can learn faster if I do something 200 times, 300 times in 6 months. I will not be able to learn if I just do it two or three times.

## Magnitude vs. Duration Moves — Holding Trades Longer

**Host:** Is holding trades for longer something you had to develop a skill set for? The patience to let your trade play out, not micromanaging, not moving the stop loss too aggressively?

**Pradeep:** It's a skill set, but more than a skill set, you have to have a **setup where you can hold it for a longer term**. I have a specific setup of turnaround stocks where I know based on their history that it can be held for a little longer than a growth stock.

Fundamentally, there are **two types of moves** in the market:

| Move Type | Characteristics | Duration |
|-----------|----------------|----------|
| **Magnitude Move** | Speed/velocity is very rapid; big magnitude (100–200%+ move) | Short |
| **Duration Move** | Stock keeps going up for months or years, but velocity may not be very high | Long |

The problem for many traders is they want to hold for longer term, but they are getting into trades which are magnitude moves. Here's a very simple observation:

> **Fast moves tend to mean revert. Slow moves tend to persist.**

You can't have a fast-moving stock and hold it for a longer term — it just doesn't work. You have to start with a setup which is designed for longer-term holding.

Intellectually, it doesn't appeal to me to hold something longer term — you get addicted to short-term profits as a trader. But if I have to do longer-term trading, I will base it on a setup which is very analysis-based, which is based on valuation, on projecting how many quarters in a row that stock is likely to have growth. The setup per se is the most important thing. Just forcing yourself to hold for longer term and saying "I'm going to put a stop at the 10-day moving average and let my trade run" is not going to help if the underlying setup isn't designed for a longer-term hold.

## Build Your Playbook — Copy Before You Create

**Host:** Is there a large gap in what traders are doing — are they not going deeper into their setups, creating different playbooks with different rule sets?

**Pradeep:** A good chart itself is not a setup. You have to find a chart which is good, and there has to be some reason why the stock is going to go up. Stocks go up for a reason — they don't go up just because there is a pretty good chart or support or resistance. You have to trade stocks which have a reason to go up. That reason might be a theme, a sector, earnings, a catalyst, a story — but that particular stock should have a reason to go up.

If you are not profitable at that stage, you are just stuck in the mud and nothing makes sense. The traders who develop these edges, who expand their edges, are profitable. Once you are profitable, you have the luxury of creating multiple edges or creating a playbook with multiple plays.

> **The first task for a trader is to become profitable.**

How do you become profitable? I always tell people: **copy somebody**. Copy some trader who is profitable and do what he does. Don't reinvent the wheel. First, make something which is a formula work, and then do your own thing.

I started not by creating my own playbook. I found a book when I was just starting trading — *Hedge Fund Edge* by Mark Boucher. He had a short-term trading setup detailed in that book. I took that, made it work as-is without making any changes. Once I made it work for 2 years, then I improvised and created a setup of my own around it.

So in the beginning, you need to copy somebody — and that's the easiest way. Actors copy others. Businesses copy others. Everybody does this. Rather than go out there and try to create something from scratch, especially off the back of not having any results or a process, take something that's known to work and has the data and the results. Replicate that, and then through doing that process, you're going to develop your own process, develop discipline, develop the skill set and the knowledge and awareness of the market. Build your reps up to a point where then you can start to make tweaks or make it your own.

## The Episodic Pivot Playbook — Common Mistakes

**Host:** By now you've come across thousands of traders who have used EPs and that playbook. Is there anything that stands out — anything they struggle with, anything they potentially do wrong in the beginning?

**Pradeep:** The problem for a lot of new traders who start trading any method — whether it is EP or any method — is that their **profit expectations and their win rate expectations are not in line with the reality** of what professional traders are dealing with.

There was an example just 3 days ago. A stock — I think the ticker was YTX — went up 30 or 40% in a day. There was a trader who bought it right at the beginning of the day. We had discussed it in a pre-market meeting. He didn't take profit. The next day, the stock ended up giving up all the gains.

He's crying. I said, "Look, what did you expect? If a stock made 20% from your entry in a day, what were you expecting — that there was going to be a miracle and you were going to buy an island? Take that profit."

These are things which professional traders have learned through experience. If you get a home run kind of a thing — 1 hour, 2 hours — my first reaction is to just lock in some profit. I'll keep a smaller position and see what happens. That kind of learning is difficult for new people because they're thinking very differently. Their real thinking is, "X has gone up 20%, in 1 month it was going to go up 200%."

> **Key Insight:** A lot of traders start pre-planning how the trade should go, how much it should make them, and the potential — and they lose sight of the process when they're in the trade and it's starting to move.

## Execution Is the Real Edge

**Pradeep:** There is no edge in an idea. Episodic pivots are not going to make you profitable until you find **execution tactics**. The process and the execution is the edge.

In day trading also, shorting small caps or stocks in play as news-based plays is not a secret. The difference between somebody who makes a million dollars in a trade versus somebody else is their **execution**. You can take a generic set of ideas and convert it into a highly profitable strategy by creating execution edges.

I see this time after time. When I see a trader who's successful, people think there is something special — he might have some extra leg or head or something. But it's their minute execution, something you would not have thought about, the way they do it, which makes the same idea very unique.

It's the same thing in the restaurant industry. I went to London a couple of months ago. A member took me to a Mediterranean restaurant — a Lebanese restaurant. He said, "I'm from Mediterranean, but this is one of the best Mediterranean foods you'll get. You won't even get this in Lebanon." And yes, it was. What's so special? They're using the same ingredients, everything. It might be just the small tweak, the small execution which they are doing differently. It's the same thing in trading.

**Host:** Do you find that over the long term, it's only very small details that make the biggest difference?

**Pradeep:** It's all very minute details, small things — in terms of entry techniques, execution. Most of the trading problems people have found solutions to. One problem I encountered a lot in the first few years: a lot of my trades used to make 20–30% gains, and then I used to think, "This is 20–30%, so it will keep going up" — which is exactly what new traders think. I would end up closing the trade break-even or just when it started reversing because I was giving them room to run.

Then I realized one of the best ways is to **sell into strength**. That's what all professional traders realize after going through this kind of period. Now, what I do: if I get into a trade and it makes 10, 12, 14, 15, or 20% in just two or three days, **I sell 80% of my position and keep 20%**.

That solution — if I had found it in the first 10 years, I would have kept so much of my money.

> **Execution Rule:** When a trade makes 10–20% in 2–3 days, sell 80% of the position and keep 20%.

## Master One Setup Before Expanding

**Host:** Do you think a large part of mastering trading is being able to segment the different areas — strategy, execution, risk management, fundamental analysis — and master each one rather than trying to learn everything at once?

**Pradeep:** You have to take it in bite-size pieces, and you have to trade **one setup idea** if you're new. If you try to expand your playbook too early — I only traded one setup idea for 10 years. Just one setup idea. And I didn't even have a variation of that also. So then you become expert in it.

You have to trade one set of ideas for a long period of time, which is true in any other field. Before I came to the US, I was in Bombay. There was one guy who was a camera repair expert — only doing camera repair, and only for certain brands of camera. People used to come from all over. You find people who specialize in something.

One of the problems with Twitter and YouTube is you get exposed to so many different styles of trading. You feel like you're in a Chinese buffet — you need to eat this, you need to eat that. As a result, you overload your plate.

> **Timeline:** It takes almost 3 to 6 months to make one setup idea work. Sometimes 6–7 weeks, sometimes 6 months to get just the entry techniques right or exit technique right.

If I change my setup every day, every week, every month, I never build expertise. But you can't blame new traders — they don't know what will work for them. So they have to try different things, but try without losing money.

**Host:** One of the biggest things that hold traders back is during that testing period, they're too quick to throw money into trading. Do you think more traders would have longevity if they could really reduce how much they're willing to put in during a testing phase and focus purely on developing their skills?

**Pradeep:** Yes, that's exactly what I tell anybody who comes to me. If you want to learn this setup, start with 5 shares or 10 shares. Doesn't matter — you might make only $20 on that trade. But make it consistently for 6 weeks, 3 months, and then go to 40 shares, 100 shares, 300 shares.

Do they want to hear this? No. Because they want to start trading today, tomorrow buy a Ferrari, have a boat. And in the process, they lose their account.

When I personally try some new trade ideas or setup ideas, I start by putting only 20 or 30 shares to test the idea. I don't want to lose money — because if you lose your stake, the game is over. And that's what happened to a lot of traders. Many times, traders are just on the cusp of becoming profitable, but then they run out of money.

> **The Coke Analogy:** Most profitable traders only trade one or two setups. They might have variations, but fundamentally — there is Coke, and there is Cherry Coke, and there is Vanilla Coke. They're all basically the same thing. Delayed EP is just a slight twist on EP — it's not a new setup.

## Self-Leadership — The Differentiating Trait

**Host:** What do you look at in terms of a correlating factor for traders?

**Pradeep:** One thing I look at in a trader is **leadership**. If they don't show leadership in any aspects of other things, I don't think they will make it in trading. All the successful traders are leaders — also in personal life, or in the way they approach it. The personality type which is required to be profitable needs to be a leadership-type personality.

But it's **self-leadership** — not necessarily leading others. Self-leadership means: if I don't have an answer, I go and find it. When I was a beginner trader, I used to define a problem and go for a walk — 2, 3 hours. I'd come out thinking, "What can be the possible solution? Solution one, solution two, solution three."

As against that, if I just sit there and wait for answers to come in, I don't have self-leadership. In my group, a lot of members have a trading problem and the first thing is, "Hey, can I have a meeting with you?" They don't even make an attempt to find a solution first.

When Christian Kulami joined StockBy, he went through the history, read all the past posts, and just figured things out. That ability — self-leadership — is the most important variation I've seen between successful and unsuccessful traders.

**Host:** Would you say as well that part of self-leadership is having a good tolerance for time horizon — understanding the realistic journey?

**Pradeep:** I think it's also like how you start your trading journey is going to determine that. A lot of younger people nowadays can start in a proprietary firm. If you're getting into a proprietary firm, your pathway to getting profitable can be significantly different — you already have a role model, you can see what works, and you're starting with a certain foundation. If a new trader sitting in his basement is trying to become profitable, his pathway is going to be different, and he has many challenges ahead.

## Creativity Before Discipline

**Pradeep:** If you talk to traders who are successful, after they are successful, they say they're very disciplined. But **discipline doesn't make you a successful trader**. What makes you a successful trader is **creativity and innovation** — because when you have a problem, whether entry or exit, you need to iterate and creatively find a solution.

You'll see when you interview traders — they have their own unique way of doing it. They found they were creative and innovative enough. But once they find it, *then* they become disciplined.

> **The Paradox:** If you are very disciplined in the beginning, you'll never go outside the box. You'll never be innovative and creative. You'll never be able to solve problems — because the fundamental problem for a beginner trader is to solve your own trading problem. To solve problems, you need creative innovation.

That's another element I see in successful traders: if something doesn't work, they have that tinkering ability to hustle and find a workable solution — not an ideal solution.

**Pradeep:** It was an accidental entry. I came to the US in 1998, and as soon as I came, there was a .com bubble. Everybody was trading. I joined a .com startup — the person behind the idea was a trader. I was just watching him; I wasn't interested in trading at that time.

We were working on this startup, and I had to go to California and stay in his house because we were hustling for raising money. The first trade that really changed everything: I had become profitable — not making big money, but making good money to make a living, doing swing trading. One night I was reading a book, and there was just one paragraph around 11:00 PM. The author said: when you have stocks that come out with earnings and the earnings are phenomenally good — 300, 400, 500% — then those stocks can double or triple.

I bookmarked that page before going to sleep. Next morning I wake up. At that time, *Investor's Business Daily* was a physical newspaper delivered to you every day at 6:00 AM. I opened the newspaper — it had the list of stocks that released earnings the night before. There was this small stock called USLB (US Laboratories). Sales growth was 900% and profit was 2,600%.

I said, "This author said this can double or triple." So I put all my money in that trade. In less than 6 weeks, I made more money than I had ever imagined in my life in one trade. That became the EP idea. From there, I started hunting for trades like that and systematized a way to find them.

## Winning Streaks, Losing Streaks, and the God Syndrome

**Host:** Have you ever noticed that winning streaks in your trading are followed by losing streaks?

**Pradeep:** Now that I'm much wiser — I've been doing this for many years and I self-analyze — I've seen that whenever you're in a good streak, when you're making money, you start getting this **god syndrome**. You think you're gifted to trade. I also see interviews of traders who made big money — they talk as if they figured it out. And the market will invariably teach you a lesson once you get that god kind of syndrome.

> *"It's a heady feeling when you make half a million, a million very quickly — that you start believing your own bull."*

The moment I have that feeling, or I make more than what I think, I have an internal clock — so I reduce my trading. I write down on a post-it note in front of me: **"Be very careful."** Because invariably, drawdowns come in after a good strike.

There is some self-regulatory mechanism. I walk away from the computer or walk away from trading for some time just to reset myself. And same way, when you go through a lean period, it's terrible. You feel like this is never going to work.

I've been doing this for 25–26 years now, and I'm telling you: **it is never easy.** Every year you start with a fresh challenge. Every month you start with a fresh challenge. You are not guaranteed that you're going to make money. Ever.

## The Four-Factor Performance Model

When I'm going through a losing phase, I always have this four-factor model for analyzing my performance:

### 1. Setup Factor

Is the setup itself wrong? If you're trading a right setup, why is the setup not working?

### 2. Process Error

Did you make a process error? Sometimes it happens. One day I was trying to put in an order for 3,000 shares and I accidentally put in 30,000 shares. I lost money and it took almost a month to recover from that big loss.

### 3. Market Factor

Most of the time, if you have a setup and a process and you're not making setup or process errors, it's **the market**. The market is not in your favor. Situational awareness becomes important.

### 4. Trader Factor

You can have a setup, you can have a process, but your own motivation ebbs and flows depending on personal circumstances. A couple of years ago I was going through a divorce — it affects your trading. For me, now, motivation becomes a bigger and bigger problem because I'm 62. I've made my money. Some days it's difficult to get motivated — what am I going to do with the money? You have the ability to trade, but you just don't have the fire in the belly. Same fire you had when you were starting with nothing.

I've seen this with a lot of traders who become successful. Human tendency when you become successful is to go splurge on something, take a holiday, buy a house. In that process, your attention gets diverted and trading suffers.

> **Reality Check:** It's never easy. The good traders who continue to do it for a long period of time are extraordinary in terms of motivation. They are unique in terms of keeping the flame alive.

## Breaking Bad Habits

**Host:** How often have you found it to be a real challenge for people to undo bad habits they've built up over 2–3 years?

**Pradeep:** It's very difficult once you build bad habits to change them. There's procedural memory — if you learn the wrong way to drive, some people ride the brake once they learn, and it's very difficult to change. Same way in trading.

It can be done, but you need very high self-leadership. It's the same thing as losing weight or fitness. In the last five years, I've lost 60 pounds. I go to the gym every day, work out every day. That is self-leadership and self-discipline.

To change something which is wrong, first there has to be a realization: "What I'm doing is wrong." Then you have to remind yourself every day to change that. It becomes difficult. Unfortunately, most people lose the money by the time they figure it out.

### The Rock Bottom Story

I had an example in StockBy, many years ago. One guy wrote a long email — his "sad story of life." He started trading, made some money, got overconfident. He had a girlfriend — asked her to give him $200,000. He put all of that money in and lost it. Lost his girlfriend, had nothing. He said, "Can you help me?"

I said, "How can I help you? I can't give you money. What I can give you is a free membership to StockBy, and I can talk to you multiple times whenever you have a problem."

This guy was so desperate that in 6 months, he developed a method to trade and went on to make big money and became very successful. But before he became successful, he had reached rock bottom. It's like alcoholics — one day they hit rock bottom and say, "This is it. I'm going to be a better person."

But the worst is people who have a lot of money — doctors, dentists. They never want to learn because they say, "Okay, I lost this much. I can make it up in my practice." That's a very dangerous game.

## Following the Money — What's in Play
