## Introduction — The Trade That Got Away

A Friday morning in late January. The market had been up two straight days, and the overnight Globex surge made a higher open almost certain. The author sold five S&P contracts on the open — the third day up, a high-probability short. By 9:37 A.M. the market was dead in the water, so he bailed. Then it broke hard and never looked back. The five-lot was worth $18,000 at the low. He had none of it.

After lunch, with the market churning sideways, he noticed something on the **five-minute chart**: a classic failure swing to the downside followed by a sharp reversal — a textbook setup for the late-afternoon rally into Friday's close. He and his trading partner loaded up. They exited near the top. The profit was a few hundred dollars shy of $10,000 — not the $18,000 left on the table, but a respectable end to the week.

The lesson: the market is not random. It **tips its hand** to those who know where to look. That is what this book is about.

The pitfalls are many. **Not everyone understands the sizable downside risk.** No one enjoys losing money, yet all traders lose money at some time or another. Managing risk is at the heart of trading.

> A 60 Minutes episode once profiled novice traders who, after losing their money, blamed market professionals and continued losing long after reasonable people would have quit. The sad truth: **most people get what they want from the market.**

**The paradox of professional trading:**

- More importantly, the day-to-day market experience is a continuous battle between **yourself and your emotions.**
- An old-boy network on the floor still exists — veterans aren't receptive to sharing with newcomers.
- But once the new trader stands his ground for six months, credibility is established.

**The key insight:** *While there is a propensity to blame a host of villains for one's difficulties in the market, the real struggle, at bottom, comes down to you against yourself.*

## 1.7 Time and Price — The Breakthrough

The real breakthrough came when the author discovered a way to quantify what can only be described as **intuitive trading.** The master intuitive trader knows *before* she knows where the market will go.

**The Paradoxical Event:**

> Most traders are most cautious when they should be most bold, and vice versa. When you catch a winner, you think the sky's the limit; when the market is about to run, you are scared to death, so you let a good opportunity slip away.

**W.D. Gann's market symmetry** was the foundation. There was a pattern in the market, and it was right there in front of the author.

> *Rallies are followed by profit-taking market breaks just as declines are followed by bargain-hunting rallies. The price pattern often exhausts itself right where it started.*

**Three key questions a trader needs answered:**

| Question | What It Tells You |
|---|---|
| **Where is the market going?** | The price target for the move |
| **When will it get there?** | The time target for the move |
| **Where am I in trouble?** | The point of maximum adversity (stop) |

> *If the symmetry fails, it signals a market reversal.* The time-and-price method is most effective when you've found the genuine trends of the day — usually one in the morning and one in the afternoon — not one of the many gyrating crosscurrents.

## 1.8 Daily Patterns

Some of the author's most successful trades have come from anticipating the day's trend.

**The fundamental research findings (13+ years of S&P 500 data):**

- **Thursday is the weakest day of the week** — if you have just one day to sell, sell on Thursday (in a bull market). In a bear market, Thursday is the countertrend day.
- **Monday is the strongest day of the week** — to buy the stock market or S&P 500 stock index futures.
- **Wednesday is the choppiest day** — typically the search-and-destroy pattern.
- **Friday is the reposition day** — late-afternoon rallies into the close are common.

**The four vital indicators:**

1. **Pivot breakout buy and sell numbers** — signal key breakout points.
2. **5-day average range** — tells you whether the current range is on target.
3. **5-day oscillator** — measures overbought/oversold conditions.
4. **Day-of-the-week overlay** — relates the indicator reading to a specific day.

> The real art comes in how you put all these indicators together.

# Chapter 2 — Time and Price: Sniper Trading

> **"Sniper trading is a hit-and-run approach to the market, in which time and price play the key roles."**

Sniper trading is based on the notion that a specific pattern or **symmetry** can be identified and quantified. Learning to measure market trends in terms of time and price is the basis of successful trading using this approach.

**The ideal pattern:** two legs in perfect symmetry completing one trend. The first leg forecasts the magnitude and duration of the second leg. Ideally, there are **two trends a day** — one in the morning and one in the afternoon.

**The hierarchy of symmetry:**

| Scale | What It Comprises |
|---|---|
| **Leg** | The smallest unit of a trend |
| **Trend** | Two legs in symmetry |
| **Day** | Two trends in symmetry (morning + afternoon) |
| **Week** | Two weeks comprise half a month's trend |
| **Month** | And so on, outward |

> The market might fail in time but not in price, and vice versa. You must settle for **partial symmetry** and identify the longer-term objective — even when exiting on a tick-by-tick basis.

**Three foundational approaches to market analysis:**

1. **Time and price** — measures the amount of time it took the market to reach level A or level B.
2. **Support and resistance** — measures key price levels.
3. **Day-of-the-week patterns** — factors in the specific day for a market bias.

> The author met "Bill" — a former student who had mastered time-and-price symmetry — and spent an entire week identifying patterns. By 10:44 A.M. on the very first morning, the December S&P 500 traded at exactly the price Bill had predicted. The trend was exactly eight minutes long. *"The market told me," Bill said.*

---

## 2.1 What Is Sniper Trading?

> **Sniper trading applies to both aggressive and conservative temperaments.** To keep the risk small, you have to focus on small moves.

**What is a "small move"?** It's relative. When the S&P 500 contract first started trading in April 1982, 3.00 points was a good day. Today the average range is approximately 10x that amount.

**Position sizing at $250 per handle:**

| S&P Move | Profit on 4 Contracts |
|---|---|
| 4.00 points | $1,000 |
| 4.00 points (E-mini S&P) | $200 |

**The beauty of sniper trading:** It is not what you stand to make but what you have to risk that determines a good trade. This style keeps the risk both manageable and small — the two vital ingredients of a good risk-reward equation.

**Three keys to success:**

1. **Don't ask too much of the market.** The more you want, the more risk you must be willing to undertake.
2. **Be patient.** You can capitalize on trends, but if impatience drives you to reckless trades, you'll be demoralized when genuine opportunities occur.
3. **Use confirming indicators.** Couple divergence techniques with the time of day to capitalize on the very best trades.

**Embracing the unknown:** Learning to embrace uncertainty in a world filled with experts is a necessary but difficult task for the new trader. Trying to eliminate risk is like trying to capture running water in a sieve. **Once the certainty exists, the opportunities evaporate.**

> A person once offered to make the author 100% on his money in a week, with no risk — for a $100,000 payment up front. *No such animal exists.*

## 2.2 The 17 Rules of Time-and-Price Trading

> The core of the Sniper Trading methodology. Each rule is a discrete discipline to be applied in sequence.

### Rule 1 — Measure the First Leg

1. **Measure low to high** (or vice versa).
2. **Measure elapsed time** of the move in minutes.

**Example:** The market moved **8.00 points higher in 14 minutes.** The implication is that the market will mirror this pattern on a second leg.

### Rule 2 — Establish the Equilibrium Point

The equilibrium point is defined as **two consecutive closes at the same price** following the first leg of a trend. It is the point where supply and demand come into balance following a rally or decline.

> Wait until an equilibrium level is established before you take a trade. Equilibrium levels are easier to identify on one-minute bars. If you can't see one, you probably haven't waited long enough — the complete consolidation may take 15–20 minutes to form.

### Rule 3 — Calculate the Profit Objective

**Add the first leg to the equilibrium point** if the market is rallying. **Subtract** if declining. The second leg should be identical in length to the first.

| Market Direction | Calculation | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Rallying** | Equilibrium + First Leg | 1309.00 + 7.00 = **1316.00** profit objective |
| **Declining** | Equilibrium − First Leg | 1309.00 − 7.00 = **1302.00** profit objective |

### Rule 4 — Calculate the .618 Retracement (Point of Maximum Adversity)

> The **.618 retracement** is the maximum amount of adversity one wants to sustain on a trade prior to exiting at a loss.

**Formula:**

- **Rallying market:** High of the first leg − (First leg × 0.618)
- **Declining market:** Low of the first leg + (First leg × 0.618)

**Example (rising first leg of 8 points, high at 1300):**

> .618 retracement = 1300.00 − (8.00 × 0.618) = **1295.06**

> The .618 level creates a **buy or sell zone** and provides a **point for stop placement.** The best buy positions attract aggressive buying well before the .618 level is reached.

### Rule 5 — Buy or Sell at or Near the Equilibrium Price

- **Buy zone:** From the equilibrium price **down** to the .618 retracement.
- **Sell zone:** From the equilibrium price **up** to the .618 retracement.

> **Critical warning:** Waiting for the market to break out is a highly risky strategy. At that point, the market is poised to run and you probably haven't sufficient time to jump aboard without sustaining substantial risk. If you don't believe it, try it and see for yourself.

### Rule 6 — Track All Trades on One- and Five-Minute Bars

You'd be surprised how different a one- and five-minute chart might appear. You need **at least two charts of different time increments** to zoom in and out on the market.

> Concentrating solely on one-minute charts leads to trying to capture minor moves when your attention should be on finding the two major trends a day.

### Rule 7 — Calculate When the Second Leg Should End — to the Minute

This is tricky because you don't yet know when — or if — the market will break in your direction. You have to take every significant intraday low inside the consolidation and measure from that moment forward.

**Example:** A low forms at 2:07 P.M. East Coast time. The seven-minute rally should end at precisely **2:14 P.M.**

> *I know from experience that not trying to anticipate the top is a big mistake. Once the tide turns, the exuberant buying will turn to panicked selling. Far better to sell into a jubilant crowd of buyers just before someone turns out the lights.*

### Rule 8 — Time vs. Price Quandary

What if the trade works in time but not in price (or vice versa)?

> You have to make one of those **difficult judgment calls** using common sense. The one thing you don't do is think about the money.

### Rule 9 — Pay Attention to the Rule of 3

> **The Rule of 3 states that a significant penetration of a support or resistance will often occur on the third attempt.**

By knowing when the market should run, you allow yourself to get positioned for the subsequent move. Used in this fashion, the Rule of 3 becomes a **timing technique.** Should the pattern fail to penetrate on the third attempt, you can often anticipate a market reversal.

### Rule 10 — Always Buy or Sell Inside the Consolidation

There is no excuse for breaking the rule. If the market starts to run before you have an opportunity to take your position, **let it go.** There will always be another opportunity in the next trading session.

> The normal tendency is to want to chase the market once a breakout occurs. This is almost always a mistake. The first leg is always a precursor to the second leg.

### Rule 11 — Use Confirming Indicators

> Confirming indicators are the **icing on the cake** — added confirmation that you are on the right side. The ones that work best are those that give a **market divergence.**

**Slow Stochastics** — The author's preferred indicator:

- A **divergence** occurs when prices are still declining but the stochastics fail to decline in parallel — they may even start to rise.
- This is the surest sign that the market has probably seen its lows.
- Conversely, at tops, prices may be soaring but slow stochastics fail to make new highs.

**Four S&P 500 indicators tracked simultaneously:**

| Indicator | What It Measures |
|---|---|
| **S&P Cash Price** | The underlying index |
| **Dow Jones Industrials** | Broad market sentiment |
| **TICK** | Rising − declining issues on the NYSE |
| **Premium** | Difference between nearby futures and cash index |

> The interesting thing is that **no single indicator serves best as the leading indicator.** One day, the cash may be the key; the next day, the TICK or premium might provide the clue. The **hidden clues of the market** appear as divergences and tell you when the first bottom leak occurs, not just when the gunnel is awash with water.

### Rule 12 — Trade the Continuation of the Overnight Trend

**Two ways to look at this trade:**

1. **As a continuation of a prior-day leg** — The market rallied into yesterday's close. Today's open is down, and the market consolidates, creating an equilibrium point shortly after the open. The second leg's completion will probably be the first trend of the day.
2. **As a gap trade** — When the market opens sharply higher, the market sold off into yesterday's close. The next day opens higher, then continues. The general rule: **75% of the time the gap will be filled.**

### Rule 13 — Never Place the Stop at the .618 Retracement

> While the .618 is the maximum adversity, it is **also a target for the stoprunners.**

**How to handle the .618 approach:**

- Does the market violate the .618? If so, does it **reject** this price?
- **Price rejection** — characterized by a rapid reversal in prices — occurs when stops are run.
- Use the **Rule of 3:** If the .618 is violated twice and rejected, the third attempt is the deciding factor. If rejected, **load the boat in the opposite direction.** If successful, sell new lows or buy new highs.
- **Keep an eye on time:** The market spends only 5–10 minutes searching for stops at the .618. If it violates and stays violated, **run immediately.**

### Rule 14 — Use MOC (Market-on-Close) Orders

If your target exit price is scheduled to occur in the final ten minutes of trading, **get out MOC.**

> The market occasionally becomes quite irrational and panic-driven late in the day as those on the losing side find themselves with nowhere to go. Short-sellers will panic and buy in a rush to exit. Buyers will panic and sell contracts to avoid additional losses overnight.

> *If you find yourself on the wrong side of the market going into the close, **get out immediately!** Because hope springs eternal, your like-minded buyers or sellers will wait until the final moments to panic in unison, driving prices against you and resulting in larger losses.*

### Rule 15 — Look for Price Reversals When the TICK and TICKI Get to Extremes

> The **TICK** = number of rising issues on the NYSE minus the number of declining issues.
> The **TICKI** = same calculation, but only for the 30 Dow Jones Industrial stocks (max +30, min −30).

**TICK / TICKI Reversal Rules:**

| Indicator | Buy Zone | Sell Zone |
|---|---|---|
| **TICKI** | Below −24 | Above +24 |
| **TICK** | Below −600 to −800 | Above +600 to +800 |

> **Fade high and low readings.** This is a good confirmation tool for time-and-price trades.

### Rule 16 — Pinpoint Stoprunning Areas

If the time-and-price target is just below, at, or just above a prior intraday high or low, **chances are the end of the short-term trend will be within this general area.**

> Stops tend to be clustered above and below both intraday and interday highs and lows. As a result, when prices trade into these areas, there will be heightened volume. **Once the stop orders are executed, the market often reverses.** This does not mean the termination of the overall trend, only that the immediate short-term trend is likely to stop at these areas.

### Rule 17 — The Market Speeds Up in the Afternoon

> The afternoon trend tends to go at **twice the rate of the morning move.** A trend that takes one hour to complete in the morning is accomplished in one-half hour in the afternoon — the same move in half the time. You make the profits in half the time, with less time in the trade, less time for something to go wrong, and quicker adversity if you get in on the wrong side.

**Three corollaries:**

1. **The market may have been down all morning.** In a bull market, this provides a good buying opportunity — the afternoon trend will reflect this buying.
2. **In a bear market, a morning rally provides the same opportunity** for aggressive sellers to push down prices in the afternoon.
3. **The best trending cycles vary day to day.** Look for the most recent patterns to identify whether the morning or afternoon presents the best trends.

# Chapter 3 — Support and Resistance: How to Identify Buy and Sell Zones

No method of pattern recognition is more popular than trying to identify **support and resistance zones.** From the earliest days of technical analysis, traders have been trying to quantify market moves and make sense of market patterns.

**A word of caution:** The successful analyst needs to know how to **interpret** these numbers, understand the nuances of the market, and perhaps, on some occasions, when to throw out the numbers altogether. Market behavior cannot be reduced to a number or series of numbers.

> The only way to disabuse yourself of common ailments is to accept that you just don't know every answer in advance — the old notion of **embracing uncertainty.**

---

## 3.1 The 3-Day Cycle (Taylor's Book Method)

In the 1950s, George Douglas Taylor used a strategy to track the grain market based on a **3-day cycle** that the market was engineered from within.

**The three days:**

| Day | Pattern | What Happens |
|---|---|---|
| **Buy Day** | Low made first near the prior day's low | Market rallies |
| **Sell Day** | Lots of trading activity near the prior day's high | Used by Taylor to exit the buy-day position |
| **Short-Sell Day** | High made first | Opportunity to sell before the market breaks; the market is then engineered lower to provide another buying opportunity |

> *By knowing about this 3-day cycle, you can often identify the kind of day you will have in advance.* By the third day up, all the indicators are looking positive. The cautious buyers on the sidelines want to buy as well. **That's what causes all the bullish enthusiasm on the opening of the third day up. And that's precisely where the smart money is selling.**

**The four sell-envelope numbers** (resistance / sell-side):

| # | Component | Definition |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Rally Number** | Today's low + average rally over the past 3 days |
| 2 | **Buying High Number** | Today's high + average buying high over the past 3 days |
| 3 | **Today's High** | The most recent intraday high |
| 4 | **LSS Pivot Breakout Buy Number** | Trend-reaction sell number — a resistance level in a nontrending market |

**The four buy-envelope numbers** (support / buy-side):

| # | Component | Definition |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Decline Number** | Yesterday's high − average decline over the past 3 days |
| 2 | **Buying Under Number** | Yesterday's low − today's low (averaged) |
| 3 | **Today's Low** | The most recent intraday low |
| 4 | **LSS Pivot Breakout Sell Number** | Trend-reaction buy number — a support level in a nontrending market |

---

## 3.2 The Sell Envelope (4 Numbers)

**Worked Example — Sell Envelope:**

Assume: Today's high = 1292.00, low = 1268.00, close = 1272.00.

**1. The Rally Number** (3-day average rally added to today's low):

| Day | Rally (points) |
|---|---|
| Day 1 | 24.00 |
| Day 2 | 19.00 |
| Day 3 | 21.00 |
| **Average** | **21.30** |

> Sell number from rally = 1268.00 + 21.30 = **1289.30**

**2. The Buying High Number** (3-day average buying-high added to today's high):

| Day | Buying High (points) |
|---|---|
| Day 1 | 3.00 |
| Day 2 | 1.50 |
| Day 3 | 6.50 |
| **Average** | **3.70** |

> Sell number = 1292.00 + 3.70 = **1295.70**

**3. Today's High** = **1292.00**

**4. The LSS Pivot Breakout Buy Number** (also known as the trend-reaction sell number):

> *This number serves two purposes — a resistance number when used in the sell envelope, and a breakout buy number when used as a trend-following signal in the "Trade for a Living" program.*

**Formula:** Pivot = (2 × Close) − Low. With 2 × 1272.00 = 2544.00, the result is **1276.00** — a resistance level.

> In a nontrending market, this same number represents strong resistance which should not be violated.

**The final sell envelope (averaged):**

| # | Component | Value |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Rally number | 1289.30 |
| 2 | Buying high number | 1295.70 |
| 3 | Today's high | 1292.00 |
| 4 | LSS pivot breakout buy | 1276.00 |
| **Average (Sell Number)** | | **1288.25** |

---

## 3.3 The Buy Envelope (4 Numbers)

Using the same hypothetical day: high = 1292.00, low = 1268.00, close = 1272.00.

**1. The Decline Number** (3-day average decline subtracted from today's high):

| Day | Decline (points) |
|---|---|
| Day 1 | 24.80 |
| Day 2 | 24.00 |
| Day 3 | 19.00 |
| **Average** | **22.90** |

> Buy support number = 1285.50 − 22.90 = **1262.60**

**2. The Buying Under Number** (yesterday's low − today's low):

> If today's low is higher than yesterday's low, the difference is **negative.** When you subtract a negative from a negative, you end up adding the average number to the day's low.
>
> Example: −8.00 ÷ 3 = **−2.70**. Subtracted from 1268.00 → **1270.70** as the next day's buying-under number.

**3. Today's Low** = **1268.00**

**4. The LSS Pivot Breakout Sell Number** (trend-reaction buy number):

**Formula:** Pivot = (2 × Close) − High = (2 × 1272.00) − 1292.00 = **1252.00**

> *This number is the point where prices will break out to the downside AND where they will find support when the market is drifting lower. Support, once violated, will become resistance.*

**The final buy envelope (averaged):**

| # | Component | Value |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Decline number | 1262.60 |
| 2 | Buying under number | 1270.70 |
| 3 | Today's low | 1268.00 |
| 4 | LSS pivot breakout sell | 1252.00 |
| **Average (Buy Number)** | | **1263.33** |

> Note: the two bottom numbers of the buy envelope are often identical — a **true now-or-never trade.** This is the point in the market where prices will find support and bounce, or reverse and break sharply.

**The anticipated daily range** = Sell number − Buy number = 1288.25 − 1263.33 = **24.92 points.** This anticipated range has proved very reliable in the author's studies over the years.

> *The general rule: buy within the buy envelope and sell within the sell envelope. A breakout above the sell envelope is a buying opportunity; a breakout below the buy envelope is a selling opportunity.*

## 3.4 LSS 1-Day Strength Index

**Definition:** A percentage number based on the relationship of the close to the overall range during one day of trading. A close at the top = 100%; a close at the bottom = 0%; a midrange close = 50%.

**Formula:**

> *(Close − Low) ÷ (High − Low) × 100*

**Example (S&P 500 March contract):**

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| High | 1315.00 |
| Low | 1296.50 |
| Close | 1301.00 |
| **Result** | **24%** (bearish — below 50%) |

> In general, a reading under 50% is considered bearish. The 5-day version smooths this single-day reading.

---

## 3.5 LSS 5-Day Strength Index

A more comprehensive gauge of recent market strength, using the 5-day high, 5-day low, and last close.

**Example calculation:**

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| 5-day high | 1351.70 |
| 5-day low | 1274.50 |
| Last close | 1294.00 |
| **Result** | **25%** (weak — sells signal favorable) |

> A low percentage reading tells you the market has been weak. Capitalize by **selling weakness.** A weak-to-lower open in such a market could signal continued weak prices.

---

## 3.6 LSS 5-Day Oscillator

> **If the percentage reading is above 70%, the market is bullish. If below 30%, the market is bearish. Readings between 30% and 70% are neutral.**

**Formula:** Based on the previous 5 trading days — one-day calculation done following the close.

**Worked example:**

| Field | Value |
|---|---|
| Highest price in last 5 days | 1317.50 |
| Lowest price in last 5 days | 1250.00 |
| **Result** | **17%** (very weak — continued eroding prices) |

**Smoothing:** Average the last three one-day readings to get the smoothed oscillator reading.

> A single day could be a one-day aberration. By averaging three days, you are less likely to be thrown off by a single day's price action.

**The smoothed reading is more indicative of a market's bullish or bearish sentiment.**

---

## 3.7 The Intraday High-and-Low Game

A time-honored floor trader strategy is to use the early range as pivot points — **buying the support and selling the resistance.** When you add up the rallies and declines, the money becomes quite significant.

> The most significant number is the **third attempt** to penetrate the support or resistance. If the market is in a hurry, it will run on the third attempt.

**The truly tricky pattern:** A **short-cover rally** that fails, or a simple one- or two-tick penetration of support or resistance that fails. This is the surest sign that the market is headed in the opposite direction.

**The psychology at work:**

- As long as the market rotates back and forth, the breakout players are on the sidelines.
- They place stops just above or below the market, designed to get them in when the market begins to run.
- These stops become **easy targets for the floor traders.**
- **The surest sign of stop-running:** the market makes a new high or low and then retreats.
- The breakout players bought up there, the stoprunners sold to them.

> *If you buy or sell a breakout and it doesn't work, you must reverse sides immediately! Don't think about the lost money. You don't have time. Just do it!*

**Key takeaway on stops:**

> At any time when you are holding a profitable trade, set up a series of targets where you might wish to take profits. **The first stop is almost always just above a prior or intraday high or just below a prior or intraday low.** With half the brokers in the country telling their clients to place stops just above or below prior highs and lows, this is a virtual sure-thing.

---

## 3.8 Fibonacci Numbers

Fibonacci numbers work extremely well with support and resistance. Take an intraday high and low and multiply by the key Fibonacci numbers. Then add and subtract these numbers from the intraday high and low.

**The three key Fibonacci multipliers:**

- **0.382**
- **0.500**
- **0.618**

> *The calculations pinpoint support and resistance levels, based on Fibonacci numbers, that are below and above the early range. With the calculations taken once a day, the numbers give you three additional support and resistance prices.*

## 3.9 The Evolution of LSS

**LSS** stands for **Long, Sell, and Sell short** — the trading program the author created based on Taylor's "Book Method."

> Over the years, the program, which began as the simple measuring of support and resistance, became quite sophisticated. As the market changed in recent years, we needed to go back and test some of the previous concepts. **What worked so well in 1982 didn't work so well 15 years later.**

**The key transformation:**

- In the early 1980s, the market was fading the moves on the notion that the market wasn't going anywhere.
- In more recent years, LSS became a **breakout system.** The notable example: the LSS pivotal buy and sell numbers now used to **buy strength and sell weakness.**

**LSS + Time-and-Price + Day-of-the-Week** form a geometrically perfect composition — a counterbalanced completion of market analysis. The author discovered that W.D. Gann's symmetry-based approach dovetailed with the support-and-resistance approach, and the time-and-price method enabled flexibility to switch sides during the day.

> *The most predictable seasonal patterns occur among stock market prices.* Over many years the patterns have been highly consistent. They are the final piece of the puzzle.

# Chapter 4 — Day-of-the-Week Trading: Why the Market Trades as It Does

> **Day-of-the-week trading will transform your fundamental assumptions, preconceptions, and beliefs.** The standard argument against short-term trading is that it is nothing more than a random gamble. But after 18+ years of tick data, the patterns are clear and unequivocal.

**The five-day personality profile (S&P 500, 12-year composite, 1987–1998):**

| Day | Personality | Tendency |
|---|---|---|
| **Monday** | The Barometer | Strongest day — early strength, midday profit-taking, late-afternoon rally |
| **Tuesday** | The Late-Rally Day | Choppy morning, strong afternoon |
| **Wednesday** | The Most Choppy Day | Least desirable — up-and-down rotation |
| **Thursday** | The Weakest Day | Highest in the morning, selloff in the afternoon |
| **Friday** | The Reposition Day | Continuation of Thursday's weakness, then late-afternoon rally |

> In a **down market,** Thursday will be the **countertrend day** and rally higher. In a **bear market,** Monday, Tuesday, and Wednesday may all be weak — creating a buying opportunity on Thursday.

---

## 4.1 The Market "Snapshot" Method

To create a **market composite chart,** take an average of a daily trend. If you take enough samples and average them, you will have a chart that illustrates how a market trades on any given day of the week.

> In the author's study, 577 Fridays were sampled. The price of the S&P 500 was captured every 30 minutes. By averaging, one number was generated per interval. Connecting the averages creates a composite Friday chart.

**The pattern is unmistakable:** when the spotlight moved day by day from Monday through Friday, the overall market pattern became evident. There was Taylor's 3-day cycle, the **buy day** pattern characterized by the down-opening followed by the up-close, the second day of continued strength, and then third-day choppiness as buyers and sellers tried to make up their minds.

---

## 4.2 Charting the Equity Curve

The testing works like this:

1. Outline your criteria for taking a trade and run the numbers — one for buying and one for selling.
2. Analyze the equity curve. Did it grow on a steady basis, or was it choppy?
3. How much money did the rule generate per year? Per month? Per trade?
4. **Filter out the less-desirable trades** to pinpoint the very best trades — those that over time return more than $700 per contract per roundturn.

> The downside: these money-making, high-probability trades are **considerably harder to find.** You have to spend a great deal of time on the sidelines, waiting for one with all the right criteria to present itself. This requires the kind of discipline that many new traders don't possess.

**The most important statistic is not total profits, but average profit per trade** — including losing trades. This is the **single number** that every trader must be concerned about.

> A starting point is to focus on **what works and eliminate marginal strategies.** Over-trading is perhaps the worst culprit when it comes to capturing a net profit. A losing trade **not taken** enhances your bottom line.

---

## 4.3 Monday — The Barometer

**Monday** is the day that **sets the tone for the week.** It is typically a harbinger of things to come. In a bull market, it is the best trending day — the day you want to be in on the open and out on the close.

**The typical Monday pattern:** prices rise prominently early in the day, followed by some profit-taking during the noon hour, and the final rally to the highs late in the afternoon.

> *If Monday's open is above Friday's close, this improved the results.* Mondays had a more pronounced bullish bias than the typical trading day. Buying the opening and selling the close on just any Monday, however, was too simplistic. We needed parameters to filter out the less-promising trades.

**Friday's close matters:** If Friday's close is weak (lower third of the range), Monday tends to be weak. If Friday closes in the middle or upper third, Monday tends to be bullish.

**12-year Monday strength distribution:**

| Friday's Close Position | Count | Percentage |
|---|---|---|
| Lower third of range | 154 | 27% |
| Middle third of range | 166 | 29% |
| Upper third of range | 256 | 44% |

---

## 4.4 Tuesday — The Late-Rally Day

Tuesdays need time to digest Monday's strength before resuming the climb to higher prices.

> *Tuesday mornings tend to be choppy.* Later in the afternoon, however, prices often rally, resulting in a nice upward trend. In Taylor's 3-day cycle, Tuesday is the **second day up.**

Unless Monday has been flat and the cycle has been pushed ahead a day, **Tuesday offers less promise as a buying opportunity — at least early in the day.** It takes time for the buying and selling to work itself out. The pure trend then occurs in the afternoon. This is especially helpful if you are a time-and-price trader because you need this good trend to make money.

---

## 4.5 Wednesday — The Most Choppy Day

**Wednesdays are, by far, the least desirable day to trade during the week.** They are characterized by choppy price action.

> Over the 12-year testing period, when stock prices rose substantially, Wednesdays had a bullish bias — opening nearer the lows and closing nearer the highs. The characteristic **up-and-down movement** of the Wednesday composite chart is unmistakable.

> *Wednesdays are difficult days for bond traders too.*

---

## 4.6 Thursday — The Weakest Day

> **If there is one good day to sell in a bull market, it is Thursday.** This is clearly the short-sell day in Taylor's view, with the high made first earlier in the day and the subsequent sell-off occurring late in the afternoon.

**Thursday is the countertrend day.** In a bull market, with a higher Monday, Tuesday, and Wednesday, you will almost always see some profit-taking by Thursday. If you are witnessing steadily lower prices, Thursday will be the **countertrend day** and rally higher.

---

## 4.7 Friday — The Reposition Day

> **Friday mornings can be a continuation of Thursday's weakness,** especially if Thursday closes near its lows. Yet once a final low is made on Friday afternoon, the market will often attract bargain hunters and rally into the close.

This is a pattern the author has seen many, many times. It can be **enormously profitable** to those who know how to capitalize on it.

---

## 4.8 How to Use Market Strength with Day-of-the-Week Trading

There is a strong correlation between market strength and day-of-the-week trading.

> **Measure overall market strength with the LSS 5-day oscillator.** When the oscillator reading is **above 50%**, we consider the market **bullish**; when the reading is **below 50%**, we consider the market **bearish.** This is the last full trading day prior to Monday's open.

**Two ways to use market strength:**

1. **1-Day Strength Index** — where the market closed on the previous day.
2. **5-Day Oscillator** — overall recent market sentiment.

> When both are in agreement on Mondays, you get the **strongest moves.** Not surprisingly, when you have both one- and five-day weakness, you tend to have declining prices — especially on a leading day such as Monday.

**The day-by-day strength dynamic:**

| Day | Strong Oscillator (>50%) | Weak Oscillator (<50%) |
|---|---|---|
| **Monday** | Rally | Lower prices |
| **Tuesday** | Pullback morning, late-afternoon rally | Holds gains, stronger run to top |
| **Wednesday** | Higher into close | Decline into close |
| **Thursday** | **Prices decline** (countertrend) | **Prices rally** (countertrend) |
| **Friday** | Selloff morning, late-afternoon rally | Continuation lower, then late bounce |

> *The strongest correlation between market sentiment and the day of the week occurs on Thursday — here the price action is reversed.* A strong market will see prices decline on Thursday. A weak market will see prices rally on Thursday.

**Why Thursday reverses:** In a rising market, buyers take profits. In a declining market, short-sellers take profits. The former push the market lower, the latter push it higher. In either case, **the trends are very clear.**

---

## 4.9 Relationship of Yesterday's Close to Today's Open

When you decide to take a trade, regardless of the day of the week, **pay attention to how far the market moves from the previous close to the next day's open.** In general, higher opens are more favorable to buying, and lower opens more favorable to selling.

**The 75% gap-fill rule:** If the market gaps higher on the open, you can sell it, knowing that you have the edge. The previous day's close will be revisited **3 out of 4 times.**

> **Beware of extreme gaps.** When the market has opened much higher or lower, chances are it won't be trading back at the previous day's close. In this instance, the market wants to go somewhere — buy strength and sell weakness.

**The 5-day range constraint:**

> Always be aware of the average 5-day range, and always be aware of how much of that range has already occurred during a given day. If 18 points of a 20-point average range have already occurred, the remaining 2 points in potential profit **aren't worth the downside risk.**

**An 8-year study result:**

| Filter Applied | Trades | Commissions | Profits |
|---|---|---|---|
| All trades | baseline | baseline | baseline |
| Drop buys opening >0.75% below prior close; drop sells opening >1.8% above prior close | **−14% fewer** | 14% fewer | **+19% more** |

> *This is the kind of information that is very valuable.*

---

## 4.10 Comparing Apples and Oranges — Percent vs. Price Charts

You'll notice that the author talks about percentage points above or below the prior close. The same percentage move on a $1,000 stock is radically different from a $5 stock.

> *Always be aware of the percentage of the range the market has already traversed on the day. This puts a frame of reference on the trade.*

---

## 4.11 Sell Trades Ranked by the Previous Close

> **We treat buy and sell trades differently.** Both are sensitive to how a market closes on the previous day. This is a **breakout system** that requires the market to trade down to a sell target before a trade is initiated.

**Day-specific sell-trade insights:**

| Day | Sell Trade Conditions |
|---|---|
| **Monday** | Far better with a strong Friday close (above 50%). Below 50%, sell signals tend to generate losses on a bounce up off the lows. |
| **Tuesday** | Sell trades do much better if Monday's close was in the higher 70th percentile — a weak day on Monday usually comes on the heels of a Friday selloff. |
| **Wednesday** | Sell trades do best when Tuesday's close is strong. Wednesday buyers benefit from this strong trend. |
| **Thursday** | Buy trades do best when Wednesday's strength is above 50%. |
| **Friday** | Best buys occur following a **bounce off Thursday's lows or a break off Thursday's high.** |

> *If the market has been in an uptrend and you get the predictable break on Thursday afternoon, bargain hunters should step in and bid up the market. If they don't, something is wrong.*

---

## 4.12 Measuring Momentum — the 3-Day Difference

> **Momentum suggests when a trend is speeding up, slowing down, or about to switch direction.** It is not enough to know whether a market is rising or falling; we must know where the turns are.

**Formula:** Today's smoothed 5-day oscillator reading minus the reading from 3 days ago.

| Reading | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| **Positive and growing** | Market moving higher with increasing strength |
| **Negative and growing** | Market moving lower with increasing weakness |
| **Large positive or negative** | Suggests a sudden big move — and an imminent reversal |
| **Slowing (smaller positive or negative)** | Suggests the trend is exhausting |

> *The turns in the market tend to occur when the numbers grow large, either positive or negative. These large numbers suggest a sudden big move. Since no trend can persist without profit-taking, a large 3-day difference suggests an imminent market reversal.*

**6-category momentum grid:**

| Oscillator | 3-Day Diff Range | Profitable Direction |
|---|---|---|
| Above 70% | Up to +28 | **Buy** (but watch for top) |
| 30%–70% | −28 to +17.5 | **Buy** |
| Below 30% | More positive than −28 | **Buy** cautiously |
| Above 70% | Greater than +3 | **Sell** (extreme bullishness) |
| 30%–70% | Less negative than −12 | **Sell** |
| Below 30% | More negative than −12 | **Avoid selling** (too weak) |

> *At market tops, the upward momentum will begin to slow before the actual top is reached. At market bottoms, the rate at which the market declines will also slow.* This is the **leading indicator** that you have an opportunity to step in and fade the move.

## 4.13 Some Simple Rules

> The author is loathe to indulge hard-and-fast rules written in stone, but here are some that work if you follow the guidelines. There are a number of filters involved, so there are only **three trades per week maximum.** On some weeks, there will be none.

**The hard-and-fast rules:**

1. **Do not trade on Tuesdays or Thursdays.**
2. **Mondays — Buy** only at the LSS pivot breakout buy number if:
   - The 5-day LSS strength number is between 65% and 94.5%.
   - The 5-day LSS oscillator is above 50%.
   - The 3-day difference in the 5-day oscillator is more positive than −10.
3. **Wednesdays — Sell** only at the LSS pivot breakout sell number if:
   - The 5-day LSS strength number is in a specified range.
   - The 5-day LSS oscillator is below 50%.
   - The 3-day difference in the 5-day oscillator satisfies a momentum filter.
4. **Fridays — Buy** only at the LSS pivot breakout buy number if:
   - The 5-day LSS strength number is below 83%.
   - The 5-day LSS oscillator is below 50%.
   - The 3-day difference in the 5-day oscillator is more positive than −40.

---

## 4.14 Beware of High- and Low-Volatility Days

**High-volatility days** are fine if you are trading them, but if you base your analysis on them, you will be **buying too high and selling too low.** Check your current range day against the 5-day average range. When the day just finished exceeds the 5-day average range, **it requires another high-range day to generate profits.**

**Low-volatility days** create the opposite problem: the breakout numbers are too close. Unless the current day you are using to perform your analysis is at least **70% of the 5-day average range,** the buy and sell numbers tend to be too close to the market.

> *I cannot emphasize enough the importance of using breakout numbers. The market is a better buy higher up and a better sell lower down. Buying or selling before these critical breakout areas are reached only increases the probabilities of sustaining a loss.*

## 4.15 Some Rules for Treasury Bonds

Day-of-the-week trading works as well in other markets as for stock index futures. **Perhaps the second-best market is U.S. Treasury bond futures** — you have the two key components needed for successful day trading: **volatility and liquidity.**

**Bond-specific rules:**

| # | Rule |
|---|---|
| 1 | Buy at the LSS pivot breakout buy number. |
| 2 | Sell at the LSS pivot breakout sell number. |
| 3 | One trade a day. |
| 4 | No breakout sell trades when the day's range exceeds 133% of the 5-day average range. |
| 5 | No breakout buy trades if the oscillator is below 25%. |
| 6 | No breakout sell trades if the oscillator is above 72.5% or below 22%. |
| 7 | No breakout buy trades if the oscillator is above 70% and the 3-day difference is greater than 7.5. |
| 8 | No breakout sell trades if the oscillator is below 30% and the 3-day difference is less than −37 or greater than +5. |
| 9 | No breakout buy trades if the previous close to the entry is greater than +14/32. |
| 10 | No breakout sell trades if the previous close to the entry is more than −6/32. |
| 11 | Don't buy on Tuesday if the previous close to the open is greater than 17% of the 5-day average range. |
| 12 | Don't buy on Wednesday if the previous close to the open is less than 21% or greater than 41% of the 5-day average range. |
| 13 | Don't buy on Thursday if the previous close to the open is greater than 30% of the 5-day average range. |
| 14 | Don't sell on Thursday if the market opens at more than 17% of the 5-day average range below the previous close. |
| 15 | Don't sell on Fridays if the open is more than 42% of the 5-day average range below the previous close. |
| 16 | **Don't sell bonds on Monday, Tuesday, or Wednesday.** |

> *The bonds, like the S&P's, are more likely to be weak later in the week.*

## 4.16 Know the Seasonal Pattern

The final piece of the puzzle. **Seasonality plays a role even in stock market or bond prices — and even in how a given day develops during a specific time of the year.**

> *Ironically, the most predictable seasonal patterns occur among stock market prices.* Over many years, the patterns have been highly consistent — back to the administration of President Truman.

**The 50-year stock market seasonality pattern:**

| Period | Tendency |
|---|---|
| **First week in January** | Lows |
| **March** | Highs |
| **August** | Secondary highs |
| **October–November** | Lows |
| **End of year** | Highs |

> *The evidence is strong that midsummer highs are traditionally followed by late-October or early-November lows — followed, of course, by a rally into the end of the year.*

> While seasonality is probably not the single key to earning profits, it is an important component of the overall pattern of market behavior. We now turn away from the numbers to the far more elusive notion of **market psychology.**

# Chapter 5 — The Psychological Game of Trading: Why Some Traders Win and Others Lose

> *How can you teach someone to be a trader? You probably cannot. But you can point out some of the pitfalls and give some suggestions on how best to cope with them.* There are two components to successful trading — one is knowing what to do and the other is doing it.

**The two components:**

1. **Knowing what to do** — there's a great deal you can learn. There are so many mistakes that novice traders make that one hardly knows where to begin listing them.
2. **Doing it** — this is where the cliches matter: don't overtrade, keep commissions reasonable, cut losses and let profits ride, become an expert in your market, embrace uncertainty and take trades before the outcome is known, embrace a disciplined approach, use a reasonable and intelligent methodology.

---

## 5.1 The Winning Attitude

> *There seems to be an inverse relationship between how new a trader is and how difficult he senses beating the market will be.* For the new guy, beating the market is a virtual sure-thing — without substantial experience, he believes he will step into the market and take money from a seasoned professional.

**The fear-and-greed spectrum:**

- **Trader A — the hotshot.** He wants to make a killing. He finds a system, the system makes money, but $1,000 a week isn't enough. He starts plunging, and the drawdowns practically clean him out. *Victim of his greed.*
- **Trader B — the fearful one.** She wants certainty before risking money. She watches on paper, sees profits pile up, takes a small position, loses three days in a row, and quits. *That's the day the system begins a legitimate winning streak.* Victim of her fear.
- **The trader who took only the losses of a winning system.** Always on the sidelines when winners occurred, always in the market on losing days. *Not bad luck — psychological profile.*

> *We are all caught between fear and greed. Trading is mostly about behavior, especially behavior while under intense pressure.*

**A weekend in Las Vegas with a veteran S&P trader:** Six friends went out. The first-time player — a guy who never played craps before but whose business was to undertake risk — watched the dice, identified a "hot trend," and nearly doubled his bankroll by the end of the weekend.

> *His success was far from beginner's luck. Standing at the craps table, he explained to me that even with the black $100 chips piled high on the table, this was strictly small potatoes compared to the high-flying S&P market. The prospect of losing money didn't bother him.*

**The first rule of money management:** You must play a game that you can win.

> *When I needed the money I always lost. It was only once I became relaxed enough not to need to win, that I could do so.*

---

## 5.2 Embrace Uncertainty

**The reality:** Trying to eliminate risk is like trying to capture running water in a sieve. **Once the certainty exists, the opportunities evaporate.**

> A trader once called the author offering to make 100% on his money in a week, with no risk — for a $100,000 payment up front. *No such animal exists.*

**The discipline of concentration:**

> The same way surgeons don't take calls in the operating room, attorneys don't answer phones in court, and pilots don't talk to anyone during takeoff and landing other than co-pilots and air traffic controllers — **professional traders cannot afford distractions.** Creating a chaotic environment may work for the casino, but not for you.

**The author stopped letting visitors into his trading area** — even well-meaning questions can break concentration when you're trading. You'd be surprised how even a moment's loss of concentration can cost you money.

**The trading diary:** One particularly good way to keep yourself honest is to keep a **written diary** of your trading activities. Just a couple of lines will do.

> *For instance: "Sold the open today and sold more ten minutes late. Market started to break. Then they rallied in a stoprunning exercise and I panicked and bought back position at the top. Must recognize this pattern and sell more next time."*

---

## 5.3 The Fear-and-Greed Spectrum

> *Trading is mostly about behavior, especially behavior while under intense pressure.* Once you get started, you'll find out things about yourself that perhaps you never knew existed.

**Self-diagnosis questions:**

- If your personality tends toward the **fear side** of the fear-greed equation, you might find yourself getting stopped out on small moves. Analyze how your fear is harming you — and stop doing it.
- If you are given to **reckless behavior,** such as not taking perfectly legitimate profits, you may be a victim of your own greed. You simply want too much.

> *Don't beat yourself up when you find yourself doing something stupid in the market. Everyone makes these mistakes. Rather, capitalize on the experience by making a mental note of how you sabotaged yourself and vow not to make the same mistake next time.*

**Consider professional help:** A therapist for several weeks or months — long enough to find out what might be holding you back from success in the market.

> *The therapist explained to me one day that I had excellent intuition about the market, but unconsciously I was telling him only one thing — get out! I didn't listen to him. The market crashed. I lost all my hard-won profits while learning a valuable lesson about listening to my inner voice of reason.*

**The intuitive trader's edge:**

> *Intuition is a difficult, if not impossible, trait to quantify, but almost any knowledge you can gain in this area can place you in good stead.* You can cultivate intuition, but only if you are willing to put in the time observing everything you can about the market. **How does it begin to manifest itself?** When you begin to see the move emerging before it does. You know when a move is over. How do you know? That's harder to explain.

**The intuitive trader doesn't dwell on mistakes:**

> *Once the bullishness becomes overwhelming, it is over. Wherever there's a crowd, you want to be against them. If you do make a mistake, you get out immediately. The intuitive trader does not move on to the next one.*

---

## 5.4 What's Holding You Back?

> *I've often said that there are a million ways to lose when trading, but only a few ways to win. When you screw up, it's normal to assume that you are fatally flawed and naturally incompetent.* It just goes with the territory.

**A prescription for disaster:** not knowing what you are doing, blaming others, becoming stubborn. **This is a trinity of mistakes that you can live without!**

**The stubbornness trap:** A trader once blamed his broker for a missed breakout. The author pointed out the breakout rally was only nine minutes long — the first three minutes were still in the consolidation and he didn't pick up the phone until the fourth. Placing the order killed another minute. By the time the order was filled, there were only two or three minutes remaining. Then panic selling sent it lower.

> *The market rarely accommodates foolish behavior by returning to the highs. They then compound one problem by creating another: they become stubborn.*

**Cultivate humility:**

> *This is a little like a guy who likes to play pickup games of touch football on weekends saying he thinks he could beat the players of the Green Bay Packers.* Cultivate a little humility and you'll be way ahead of those who think it is easy.

---

## 5.5 Make a Plan and Stick to It

**The discipline of consistency:**

> *Successful traders tend to do what works. This suggests flexibility in their approach to the market. Contrast this with a rule that is inflexible.* The tendency is to find one rule that works on a limited basis and then extrapolate out to try to make this limited rule a law of the universe.

**The author's core practices:**

- **Trade the same market day after day.** Become a specialist.
- **Use multiple confirming indicators.** No single indicator is best every day.
- **Track trades on both one- and five-minute charts.**
- **Use a trading diary** to record emotional and tactical mistakes.
- **Embrace uncertainty** — the moment you think you have all the answers, the market will humble you.
- **Take intelligent risks.** The willingness to risk is one of the many paradoxes that abound in the market.
- **Make a plan and stick to it.** The mark of a good professional trader is flexibility within a disciplined framework.

> *When you discover a way to quantify what can only be adequately described as intuitive trading, you can suspend your emotions and trade based on market probabilities, not what your fears dictate.*

**The final word:**

> *The real success usually comes long after the novice has served a challenging apprenticeship, just as it does in any profession. There is an inverse relationship between how new a trader is and how difficult he senses beating the market will be.* Cultivate a little humility. Set realistic, achievable goals. And remember: *the market is a percentage game, nothing more.*

# Key Takeaways

1. **The market is a percentage game — not a prediction contest.** No one "knows" where the market is going. The professional's edge is in process: managing risk, identifying the highest-probability setups, and executing them with discipline. Winning is the result of being profitable on the trades that matter.

2. **Traders are made, not born.** Most would-be traders fail because they simply don't know what they are doing. The skill can be learned, but only through study, repetition, and a willingness to lose money while learning. The real success usually comes long after the novice has served a challenging apprenticeship.

3. **Become a specialist.** Everyone who truly succeeds is a specialist. The professional trades one market, one method, the same spot in the pit, the same people, every day. By doing the same thing every day, you develop a grasp on the market that can alert you when something is wrong.

4. **Time and price symmetry is a real, quantifiable pattern.** Markets move in two legs per trend, two trends per day, two weeks per half-month, and so on outward. The first leg forecasts the magnitude and duration of the second leg. If the symmetry fails, it signals a market reversal. Patience, pattern recognition, and the willingness to wait for confirmation are the keys.

5. **The 17 rules of Sniper trading form a complete methodology.** The rules cascade from measuring the first leg, to establishing equilibrium, to calculating the .618 retracement, to confirming with stochastics and TICK/TICKI extremes, to exiting via MOC orders and the Rule of 3. They are not a rigid system — they are a framework that requires interpretation and flexibility.

6. **The .618 retracement is both a stop placement zone and a target for stoprunners.** Place your mental stop there, but never place a resting stop there. Monitor price rejection at the .618 and use the Rule of 3 to decide whether to reverse or run.

7. **Support and resistance form a two-envelope system.** The sell envelope (4 numbers) and the buy envelope (4 numbers) bracket the anticipated daily range. Use the LSS pivot breakout buy and sell numbers as breakout signals — buy the buy envelope and sell the sell envelope in a nontrending market.

8. **Day-of-the-week patterns are statistically robust.** Monday is the strongest day in a bull market. Thursday is the weakest and the countertrend day. Wednesday is the choppiest. Friday is the reposition day. Filter trades with the LSS 5-day oscillator and the 3-day momentum difference. The hard-and-fast rules generate at most three trades per week.

9. **The paradoxical event is the real edge.** The market is a better buy higher up and a better sell lower down. The easy trades are the losers. The difficult and impossible trades are the outstanding winners. Embrace the paradox; stop chasing certainties.

10. **Trading is mostly about behavior under pressure.** The two components are knowing what to do and doing it. The fear-and-greed spectrum, the distractions, the stubbornness, the sabotage — all the elements of the psychological game have the same answer: discipline, humility, a written diary, a specialist's focus, and a willingness to take intelligent risks when the odds favor a given outcome.

11. **Respect the money and respect the risk.** The best traders are smart about money — they don't squander hard-won capital on reckless trades. They know when to get out. They embrace the uncertainty. They accept that the market is always correct, no matter what their numbers say.

12. **The three approaches — time-and-price, support-and-resistance, day-of-the-week — complement each other.** While they are arrived at by totally different means, they are a geometrically perfect composition. The most successful traders use a combination of all three, plus seasonal patterns, plus their own intuition cultivated through years of observation.
