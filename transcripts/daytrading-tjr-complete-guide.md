## The Right Mindset: Trading Is A Skill

> **Key Insight:** The goal of trading is NOT to make money. The goal is to accurately predict with high probability where price wants to go. Money is the side effect of doing that well.

Most people enter trading thinking, "How can I make money?" This mindset leads to overleveraging, overtrading, and ultimately losing money. Instead, the correct mindset is:

- **"How can I predict where price wants to go today using confluences, strategy, and proper risk management?"**
- Money follows from the ability to predict price action — just like how a basketball player makes money by getting better at basketball, not by thinking about money.

> **Key Insight:** Think of trading like any other skill. If you pick up a basketball for the first time, you'll be terrible. Every skill takes trial and error. Day trading is no different.

**The two paths:**

| Mindset | Behavior | Result |
|---------|----------|--------|
| "I want to make money" | Placing trade after trade, overleveraging | Losing money consistently |
| "I want to predict price" | Waiting for high-probability setups, following a system | Money comes as a side effect |

## Fundamentals: Candlestick Anatomy

> **Source:** TJR — Candlestick Anatomy Lesson

### What Is A Candlestick?

A candlestick represents price movement over a specific time period. Each candle tells us **four things**:

1. **Open** — Where price started (bottom of body for green/up candle, top of body for red/down candle)
2. **Close** — Where price ended (top of body for green/up candle, bottom of body for red/down candle)
3. **High** — The highest point price reached during that period (top of the wick)
4. **Low** — The lowest point price reached during that period (bottom of the wick)

### Candlestick Components

- **Body** — The solid/shaded portion showing open to close
- **Wicks** — The thin lines above and below the body showing the high and low. Think of wicks like a snail trail — evidence that price was there before

### Time Frames

The time frame determines how long each candle represents:

| Time Frame | Each Candle Represents |
|------------|----------------------|
| 1 Minute | 1 minute of price action |
| 5 Minutes | 5 minutes of price action |
| 15 Minutes | 15 minutes of price action |
| 1 Hour | 1 hour of price action |
| 4 Hour | 4 hours of price action |
| Daily | 1 full trading day |
| Weekly | 1 full trading week |

The beauty of candlesticks is that you can see a month's worth of price action in candles, then zoom into weeks, days, 4-hour chunks, hours, 15-minute segments, 5-minute segments, and even individual minutes.

## Fundamentals: Highs, Lows, and Trends

> **Source:** TJR — Highs, Lows, and Trends Lesson

### Identifying Highs and Lows

A **high** is a two-candlestick pattern:
- A move **up**, then a move **down** (green candle followed by red candle)
- Mark the **highest point** of those two candles

A **low** is a two-candlestick pattern:
- A move **down**, then a move **up** (red candle followed by green candle)
- Mark the **lowest point** of those two candles

> **Key Insight:** Don't overcomplicate this. If there are two consecutive green candles, that's not a high. If there are two consecutive red candles, that's not a low. You need the reversal candle to confirm.

### Identifying Trends

The market moves in three ways: **up, down, and sideways**.

**Uptrend:** Higher highs and higher lows
- High → Low → Higher High → Higher Low → Higher High...

**Downtrend:** Lower highs and lower lows
- High → Low → Lower High → Lower Low → Lower High...

**Consolidation (Sideways):** No clear direction — price moves back and forth

> **Key Insight:** The easiest way to identify a trend — look at where price is going. If it's going up, it's an uptrend. If it's going down, it's a downtrend. If it's moving sideways, it's consolidation.

These trends and movements can be found on **every single time frame**.

## Confluence: Break of Structure

> **Source:** TJR — Break of Structure Lesson

### What Is A Break of Structure?

A break of structure signals a **change in trend direction**.

- **In an uptrend:** If a candle **closes below** the most recent low, that's a break of structure to the downside — the trend is now shifting bearish
- **In a downtrend:** If a candle **closes above** the most recent high, that's a break of structure to the upside — the trend is now shifting bullish

### Key Rules

- It's the **close** of the candle that matters, not the wick
- After a break of structure in an uptrend, you'll typically see a lower low followed by a lower high
- After a break of structure in a downtrend, you'll typically see a higher high followed by a higher low

> **Key Insight:** You cannot turn profitable just by identifying break of structure. This is one tool in the toolbox — it becomes powerful when combined with other confluences.

## Confluence: Liquidity

> **Source:** TJR — Liquidity Lessons

### What Is Liquidity?

Liquidity is **resting orders** — buy orders, sell orders, stop losses, take profits — at specific price levels. It exists on **every time frame** and is **required for the market to move or change direction**.

### Why Does It Matter?

Liquidity **determines where the market is drawn towards**. It acts like a magnet — price is drawn to areas where large amounts of orders are waiting to be filled.

### Where Does Liquidity Lie?

Liquidity resides **above highs** and **below lows**.

**Above highs:**
- Retail traders go long when highs get broken → buy orders
- Short sellers have stop losses above highs → forced buy orders when stopped out
- Two forms of buying pressure = massive liquidity for institutions to fill their sell orders

**Below lows:**
- Retail traders go short when lows get broken → sell orders
- Long traders have stop losses below lows → forced sell orders when stopped out
- Two forms of selling pressure = massive liquidity for institutions to fill their buy orders

> **Key Insight:** The market makers use these liquidity pools. When they see a concentration of buy orders above highs, that's their opportunity to fill sell orders and cause a reversal. This is why price often "sweeps" highs or lows before reversing.

## Confluence: Order Blocks

> **Source:** TJR — Order Blocks Lessons

### What Is An Order Block?

An order block is a **price range where large orders were able to be filled** that caused the market to change direction.

### How To Identify Order Blocks

**Bullish Order Block:** The **leg down** that causes a liquidity sweep to the downside, followed by a break of structure to the upside. The price range of that leg down is the order block.

**Bearish Order Block:** The **leg up** that causes a liquidity sweep to the upside, followed by a break of structure to the downside. The price range of that leg up is the order block.

### Why Are They Useful?

If price revisits that order block and sees rejection (buying/selling pressure), it's likely that more orders are being filled in the same direction — and price will continue moving that way.

> **Key Insight:** Order blocks are a **continuation confluence** — price doesn't have to revisit them, but if it does and you see confirmation, it's a high-probability area.

## Confluence: Fair Value Gaps (Imbalances)

> **Source:** TJR — Fair Value Gaps Lessons

### What Is A Fair Value Gap?

A fair value gap (FVG) is an **imbalance of market orders** — a price range that lacks orders in the opposite direction of market sentiment. It's identified by a **three-candlestick pattern**.

### How To Identify Fair Value Gaps

**Bullish FVG:** Mark from the **top of the first candle's wick** to the **bottom of the third candle's wick**. The gap between them (where wicks don't overlap) is the fair value gap — a zone lacking sell orders.

**Bearish FVG:** Mark from the **bottom of the first candle's wick** to the **top of the third candle's wick**. The gap is a zone lacking buy orders.

**Invalidation:** If the wicks of the first and third candles overlap, there is no fair value gap — no gap exists.

### Why Are They Useful?

When price revisits a fair value gap and you see buying/selling pressure out of it with the same market sentiment, you can assume more orders were filled and price will continue in that direction.

> **Key Insight:** Fair value gaps are a **continuation confluence**. Not every FVG will get filled — but when they do and show confirmation, they provide excellent entry points.

## Confluence: Invalidated Fair Value Gaps & BPR

> **Source:** TJR — FVG Invalidation & BPR Lesson

### FVG Invalidation

A fair value gap can be **invalidated** when price closes above (bearish FVG) or below (bullish FVG) the gap.

**Two uses:**

1. **Trade invalidation:** If an FVG in your trade direction gets invalidated, scrap the trade idea
2. **New trade confluence:** If an FVG in the **opposite** direction of your bias gets invalidated, it can serve as a confluence for entry in your bias direction

### BPR (Balanced Price Range)

A BPR occurs when a **bullish imbalance** and a **bearish imbalance overlap**, creating a highly imbalanced price range. This overlapping zone is a high-quality area where price will likely want to return and balance out.

**How to identify:** Find a bullish FVG and a bearish FVG that overlap. The overlapping zone is the BPR.

> **Key Insight:** BPR is another continuation confluence — it identifies the most imbalanced price range within a volatile move, giving you a higher-probability target area.

## Confluence: Equilibrium

> **Source:** TJR — Equilibrium Lessons

### What Is Equilibrium?

Equilibrium measures the **50% mark (halfway point)** from a swing high to a swing low (or vice versa), identifying whether price is in a **premium** or **discount** area.

### How To Identify

- **In an uptrend:** Draw from the swing low to the swing high. Everything above the 50% line is **premium** (expensive). Everything below is **discount** (cheap).
- **In a downtrend:** Draw from the swing high to the swing low. Everything below the 50% line is premium (for shorting). Everything above is discount.

### The Grocery Store Analogy

- If your favorite food is 50% off, you buy it enthusiastically — that's a **discount**
- If it's 2x the price, you're less willing to buy — that's **premium**
- Market makers want the best price possible — they look to buy at discount and sell at premium

### Why Is It Useful?

Equilibrium is a **continuation confluence**. In an uptrend, if price dips into the discount area and you see buying pressure, it makes sense that orders are being filled at a "cheaper" price and price will continue higher.

> **Key Insight:** Use the Gann Box tool on TradingView to mark equilibrium. Price doesn't have to hit equilibrium — but when it does and shows confirmation, it's a strong confluence.

## Confluence: Breaker Blocks

> **Source:** TJR — Breaker Blocks Lessons

### What Is A Breaker Block?

A breaker block is a **retracement** followed by a **break of structure** opposite to the current trend. This creates a price range that **lacks orders** from the previous trend.

### How They Form

1. Price is in a trend (e.g., downtrend with lower lows and lower highs)
2. Price retraces (moves up in the downtrend) — this retracement fills sell orders
3. Price then **breaks structure** — pushing past the retracement and continuing higher
4. That retracement price range no longer has sell orders to offer
5. If price revisits that area and sees buying pressure, it's likely to continue higher

### How To Identify

- **Bullish Breaker:** The move **down** prior to a break of structure to the upside
- **Bearish Breaker:** The move **up** prior to a break of structure to the downside

### Why Are They Useful?

Similar to fair value gaps, breaker blocks are a **continuation confluence**. When price revisits and shows rejection, it confirms that the previous trend's orders have been exhausted.

> **Key Insight:** Breaker blocks paired with fair value gaps create an extremely strong confluence zone.

## Confluence: SMT Divergence

> **Source:** TJR — SMT Divergence Lesson

### What Is SMT Divergence?

SMT (Smart Money Tool) Divergence is when **one index forecasts what another index is going to do** by showing divergent behavior between correlated instruments (e.g., ES vs. NQ, or NASDAQ vs. S&P 500).

### Rules

- **Bullish SMT:** When looking for buys, compare **lows only**. One index creates a **higher low** while the other creates a **lower low**. The index making the higher low is forecasting that the other will follow and move higher.
- **Bearish SMT:** When looking for sells, compare **highs only**. One index creates a **lower high** while the other creates a **higher high**. The index making the lower high is forecasting that the other will follow and move lower.

### How To Use

1. Establish your overall bias first
2. Look for SMT divergence on the 5-minute or 15-minute time frame (not 1-minute)
3. The index that's "lagging behind" is where you execute your trade
4. SMT can also be used to validate high time frame liquidity sweeps

> **Key Insight:** Some traders won't take a trade without SMT confirmation. It's an extremely powerful tool for validating your bias and identifying which instrument offers the best opportunity.

## Strategy Creation: Systemizing Your Approach

> **Key Insight:** The majority of traders aren't profitable with SMC/ICT concepts — not because the concepts don't work, but because they don't have a **system**.

### Why Systemize?

Without a system:
- You use different time frames every day
- You enter off different confluences every day
- You have no idea why you're winning or losing
- Journaling is guesswork

With a system:
- Every trade follows the same step-by-step process
- You can identify which specific confluence causes losses
- You can remove low-probability variables
- Your win rate improves as you optimize

### The Systemization Process

1. **Trade one pair** — Eliminate the variable of multiple instruments
2. **Trade during one session** — Eliminate the variable of different market conditions
3. **Have a step-by-step entry plan** — Know exactly what conditions must be met before entering
4. **Journal every trade** — Track which confluences work and which don't
5. **Remove what doesn't work** — If 90% of your order block entries lose, stop entering off order blocks

> **Key Insight:** Keep simplifying until your system is purely high-probability. Remove every variable that has a low win rate until all that remains is what works.

## Strategy: The Basic Step-By-Step Framework

> **Source:** TJR — Strategy Creation Parts 1-3

### Step 1: Determine Your Bias (4-Hour Trend)

- Look at the 4-hour trend
- **Uptrend** (higher highs, higher lows) = Bullish bias → Look for buys
- **Downtrend** (lower highs, lower lows) = Bearish bias → Look for sells

### Step 2: Determine Your Execution Time Frame (1-Hour Trend)

- If the 1-hour trend is **in line** with the 4-hour → Scale down to the **5-minute** for execution
- If the 1-hour trend is **opposite** to the 4-hour → Scale down to the **15-minute** for execution
- Higher time frame holds higher power — if the 4-hour says up but the hourly says down, the hourly is just in a retracement

### Step 3: Wait For High Time Frame Liquidity Sweep

- Mark out 1-hour and 4-hour highs/lows in your bias direction
- **Do nothing** until those levels get swept
- If bullish: wait for lows to be swept
- If bearish: wait for highs to be swept

### Step 4: Look For Break of Structure

- Once liquidity is swept, scale to your execution time frame (5-min or 15-min)
- Wait for a break of structure confirming the reversal

### Step 5: Wait For Third Confluence

Look for one of these to get hit:
- Order Block
- Fair Value Gap
- Breaker Block
- Equilibrium

**Do nothing until one of these gets hit.**

### Step 6: Confirm With Price Action

Wait for buying or selling pressure out of the confluence:
- For bullish: A green candle closing above the confluence
- For bearish: A red candle closing below the confluence

### Step 7: Execute

Once all conditions are met, enter the trade. Set stop-loss and take-profit. Step away.

> **Key Insight:** This strategy won't give you a trade every single day — and that's okay. Quality over quantity. Wait for the setup, don't force trades.

## Strategy: Adding High Time Frame Confluences

> **Source:** TJR — Strategy Creation Part 4

Originally, the strategy only triggered off high time frame **liquidity sweeps**. This addition allows you to also trigger off high time frame **confluences**.

### When 4-Hour and 1-Hour Are Opposing

Instead of only waiting for a liquidity sweep, you can also look for **4-hour confluences**:
- 4-hour fair value gaps
- 4-hour order blocks
- 4-hour breaker blocks
- 4-hour equilibrium

When price draws into these, scale into the 15-minute and follow the same process (break of structure → third confluence → confirm → execute).

### When 4-Hour and 1-Hour Are In Line

Instead of only waiting for a liquidity sweep, look for **hourly confluences**:
- Hourly fair value gaps
- Hourly order blocks
- Hourly breaker blocks

When price draws into these, scale into the 5-minute and follow the same process.

> **Key Insight:** Liquidity sweeps are still the strongest trigger. High time frame confluences are an addition, not a replacement. Always be open to liquidity sweeps when they occur.

## Strategy: Pre-Market Liquidity Sweeps

> **Source:** TJR — Strategy Creation Part 5

Sometimes a liquidity sweep happens **during pre-market** or before the main trading session. Here's how to handle it:

### When Pre-Market Sweep Has Already Occurred

1. Identify your bias (4-hour trend)
2. Note that a pre-market sweep and break of structure already happened
3. The odds of another sweep are low
4. Instead, target **hourly confluences** formed from that pre-market move:
   - Hourly order blocks
   - Hourly fair value gaps
   - Hourly breaker blocks
5. When price draws into these confluences at market open, scale to your execution time frame
6. Follow the standard process: break of structure → third confluence → confirm → execute

> **Key Insight:** If the market has already made its sweep before you start trading, don't wait for another one. Use the confluences created by that sweep as your trigger.

## Risk Management: Stop Losses

> **Source:** TJR — Stop Loss Lesson

### Why Stop Losses Are Necessary

Stop losses help you:
1. **Practice risk management** — They force you to define your maximum loss
2. **Not lose a massive amount of money** — They cap your downside
3. **Identify invalidation points** — The exact price where your trade idea is proven wrong

### Where To Place Stop Losses (Priority Order)

| Priority | Placement | Rationale |
|----------|-----------|-----------|
| 1st (Best) | Above/below the liquidity sweep | If the sweep gets taken out, the trade idea is completely invalidated |
| 2nd | Above/below highs/lows within the confluence | Tighter stop, but allows for some breathing room within the setup |
| 3rd | Above/below the confluence itself | Gives the most room for price to move within the confluence before invalidation |

> **Key Insight:** Choose your stop-loss placement based on your risk-to-reward ratio. If placing it above the liquidity sweep gives you a terrible R:R, consider using the highs within the confluence — but understand the increased risk.

## Risk Management: Take Profits

> **Source:** TJR — Take Profit Lesson

### Key Rules

1. **Never extend or move your take-profits** — Once set, leave them alone. Emotions will try to convince you to change them.
2. **Never move your stop-loss** — Same principle. Set it and forget it.
3. **Match your take-profit time frame to your execution time frame** — Don't set daily take-profits when entering on the 5-minute. There's too much price action between entry and that target.

### Where To Take Profits

Take profits at **high confluence areas** where price could potentially reverse against your trade:

| Take-Profit Target | Source |
|-------------------|--------|
| Draws on liquidity | High time frame highs/lows that price is likely seeking |
| Order blocks | High time frame order blocks that could cause a reversal |
| Fair value gaps | High time frame FVGs that could cause rejection |
| Breaker blocks | High time frame breakers that could cause reversal |
| Equilibrium | High time frame equilibrium levels |

### Multiple Take-Profits

Consider scaling out of your position:
- **TP1:** Closest high confluence area — exit a portion of the position
- **TP2:** Next confluence area — exit another portion
- **TP3:** Final target — close remaining position

> **Key Insight:** Look for take-profit targets on the same time frames you use for bias (4-hour and 1-hour). These are the levels where price is most likely to react.

## Forex Strategy: Session Highs & Lows

> **Source:** TJR — Forex Strategy Creation Parts 1-2

### Session Times (New York Time)

| Session | Opens |
|---------|-------|
| Asian | 8:00 PM (20:00) |
| London | 3:00 AM |
| New York | 8:00 AM |

### Setup Process

1. **Mark session opens** on your chart for Asian, London, and New York sessions
2. **On the 30-minute time frame**, mark the **highs and lows** of each session with rectangular boxes:
   - Box from the base of the candle to the high
   - Box from the base of the candle to the low
3. **Scale down to the 5-minute** and wait for price to interact with these session levels
4. When price sweeps or revisits a session high/low, look for:
   - A 5-minute break of structure
   - A third confluence (FVG, order block, breaker, equilibrium)
5. Execute with stop-loss above/below the session level

### Key Rules

- Session highs and lows act as support/resistance (liquidity zones)
- New session opens can sweep previous session's highs/lows
- Pay attention to which session a candle belongs to — the session boundary determines which session "owns" that high or low
- This strategy can yield up to 3 trades per day across all sessions

> **Key Insight:** Session highs and lows are remarkably consistent in getting respected. Price will often sweep one session's level and then drive toward the opposite session's level.

## PM Session Trading

> **Source:** TJR — PM Session Lesson

### PM Session Overview

PM session uses the **same strategy** as AM session but on **lower time frames** because price moves are smaller and slower during afternoon trading.

### PM Session Time Frames

| AM Session | PM Session Equivalent |
|------------|----------------------|
| 4-Hour (bias) | 15-Minute (bias) |
| 1-Hour (draws/execution tier) | 5-Minute (draws/execution tier) |
| 5-Minute (execution) | 1-Minute (execution) |

### PM Session Steps

1. **Identify the 15-minute trend** — This is your bias
2. **Look for liquidity sweeps** on the 5-minute or 15-minute
3. **Look for a confluence** within the 5-minute (FVG, order block, breaker, equilibrium)
4. **Execute on the 1-minute** — Wait for a 1-minute break of structure or a candle in your direction

### PM Session Characteristics

- PM session tends to sweep the "gritty, grindy" lows that formed during AM session's consolidation
- Lower volatility = tighter take-profit targets (often just 1:1 R:R)
- Best PM session time: 1:30 PM - 3:00 PM Eastern

## Scaling The Strategy Across Time Frames

> **Source:** TJR — Universal Strategy Lesson

The strategy is **universal** — all confluences (liquidity sweeps, break of structure, order blocks, fair value gaps, breaker blocks, equilibrium) exist on **every single time frame**.

### For Day Trading (Default)

- Bias: 4-Hour + 1-Hour
- Execution: 5-Minute or 15-Minute
- Confirmation: 1-Minute

### For Swing Trading (Scaled Up)

- Bias: Weekly + Daily
- Draws/Execution: 4-Hour + 1-Hour
- Confirmation: 15-Minute or 5-Minute

### For Scalping (Scaled Down)

- Bias: 1-Hour + 15-Minute
- Draws/Execution: 5-Minute
- Confirmation: 1-Minute

### Key Principles

1. **Always have a higher time frame bias** — Don't trade against the larger trend
2. **Match your take-profit to your execution time frame** — Don't set daily targets from a 1-minute entry
3. **The same steps apply regardless of time frame** — Liquidity sweep → Break of structure → Third confluence → Confirm → Execute
4. **If high time frames aren't offering setups, scale down** — Don't sit idle for weeks waiting for a 4-hour sweep

> **Key Insight:** Don't look for a copy-paste strategy that works the same way every single day. Sometimes you need to adapt by scaling your time frames up or down based on what the market is offering.

## Five Tips To Becoming A Profitable Trader

> **Source:** TJR — Psychology / Top 5 Tips Video

### Tip 1: Have A Long-Term Vision

- Trading is NOT a get-rich-quick scheme
- Think in 5-year time frames
- You will likely lose money for the first 1-2 years — that's normal for any high-level skill
- Accept that this journey will take years, not months

### Tip 2: Hard Work and Consistency (On The Right Things)

Distribute your 10,000 hours across three pillars:

1. **Psychology** — Train yourself to be robotic. Detach emotions from trades. Practice discipline.
2. **Risk Management** — Study your win rate, risk-to-reward ratios, and position sizing. Learn how to avoid losers and consistently pick winners.
3. **Strategy** — Master your edge in the market. Understand candlesticks, price action, and your specific setup.

### Tip 3: Treat Trading Like A Job (But Have Fun)

- There's a difference between having fun with your work and not taking it seriously
- Trading should feel like the best game you've ever played
- If you don't genuinely love predicting where price wants to go, trading may not be for you
- Celebrate wins for the execution, not the money. Learn from losses for the skill gap, not the money lost.

### Tip 4: Treat Trading Like A Skill

- You will be terrible at first — that's expected
- A strategy doesn't "not work" just because you can't execute it after watching one video
- Skills are built through repetition, consistency, and hard work
- Focus on the skill set, not the money — the money follows the skill

### Tip 5: Put All Your Eggs In One Basket

Don't spread yourself across day trading, crypto, drop shipping, Amazon FBA, and TikTok shop simultaneously.

**The Cup Analogy:**
1. Start with **one cup** and pour 100% of your effort into it
2. It will trickle at first — shaking your hand to fill it
3. Eventually it becomes a well-oiled machine, overflowing steadily
4. **Only then** bring in a second cup and pour the overflow into it
5. Each subsequent cup fills faster because you've already mastered the process
6. Eventually you have 7+ cups all overflowing — but it started with ONE
