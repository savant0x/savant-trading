## What Is Day Trading?

Day trading is the act of buying and selling a financial instrument within a short time frame — typically within a single day. Unlike traditional investing, where positions are held for months or years, day traders enter and exit trades within hours, minutes, or even seconds.

**Key advantages of day trading over long-term investing:**

- **Speed of returns** — profits are realized much faster than buy-and-hold strategies
- **Lower capital requirements** — you can start with as little as $50–$200
- **Profit in both directions** — traders can make money when the market goes up (buying/long) *or* down (selling/short)
- **Protection from market crashes** — unlike holding stocks through a recession, day traders are not exposed to overnight or long-term downturns
- **Minimal time commitment** — effective trading can require as little as 45 minutes to 1 hour per day
- **Low equipment requirements** — a laptop or PC with working internet is sufficient; even an iPad or phone can work

> **Key Insight:** Day trading is not a get-rich-quick endeavor. It took Juvier over three years of consistent practice and study to reach a professional level. Treat it as a skill that requires time, repetition, and discipline.

## Futures Contracts & Tickers

A **ticker** (or instrument) is the symbol representing the asset being traded. A **contract** is the full unit of that asset.

### Standard & Micro Contracts

| Ticker | Contract | Margin (1 Contract) | Tick Size | Tick Value | Micro Ticker | Micro Margin | Micro Tick Value |
|--------|----------|---------------------|-----------|------------|--------------|--------------|------------------|
| **NQ** | Nasdaq 100 | $1,000 | 0.25 | $5.00 | MNQ | $100 | $0.50 |
| **ES** | S&P 500 | $500 | 0.25 | $12.50 | MES | $50 | $1.25 |
| **YM** | Dow Jones | $500 | 1.00 | $5.00 | MYM | $50 | $0.50 |
| **GC** | Gold | $1,000 | 0.10 | $10.00 | MGC | $100 | $1.00 |

**Key definitions:**

- **Margin** — the minimum account balance required to open one contract
- **Tick Size** — the minimum price increment a ticker moves in (e.g., NQ moves in 0.25 increments: 100 → 100.25 → 100.50)
- **Tick Value** — the dollar value of each tick movement per contract
- **Micro contracts** — smaller, cheaper versions of standard contracts; recommended for beginners

> **Key Insight:** Beginners should start on **micro contracts**. They behave identically to standard contracts but at roughly 1/10th the cost and risk.

## Trading Sessions

Markets are driven by institutional traders (banks, hedge funds) operating on schedules. There are three main trading sessions (all times Eastern Standard Time):

| Session | Time (EST) | Volume Level |
|---------|------------|--------------|
| **London** | 2:00 AM – 12:00 PM | Second highest |
| **New York** | 8:00 AM – 5:00 PM | Highest |
| **Tokyo** | 7:00 PM – 4:00 AM | Lowest for US instruments |

**The London–New York overlap (8:00 AM – 12:00 PM EST)** is the highest-volume window and often the best time to trade futures instruments (NQ, ES, YM, GC).

> **Key Insight:** All futures instruments covered in this course have the most volume during **New York session**. Forex pairs like GBP/JPY and USD/JPY move most during Tokyo session.

## Types of Analysis: Fundamental vs. Technical

### Fundamental Analysis (News)

News events heavily affect markets. Major economic events — inflation reports, employment data, tariffs, GDP releases — can cause significant price movements.

**How to track news events:**

- Use **Forex Factory** (forexfactory.com) to view scheduled news events
- Focus only on **red-folder USD events** when trading futures
- Avoid entering trades **5 minutes before and 5 minutes after** major red-folder news events
- Trading during news is essentially gambling — even "good" news can cause markets to sell off

### Technical Analysis (Charts)

Technical analysis involves reading charts, candlesticks, and price patterns to predict future price movement. It is based on the principle that **price patterns tend to repeat themselves**.

> **Key Insight:** A good trader must understand *both* fundamental and technical analysis. Be aware of the news, and be skilled at reading charts.

## Candlestick Anatomy

Every candlestick tells the story of what happened during its time interval. It has two components:

- **Body** (thick part) — represents the open and close prices
- **Wick** (thin lines above and below) — represents the highest and lowest prices during that interval

### Bullish Candlestick (Green by default)

| Part | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| Bottom of body | **Open** (where price started) |
| Top of body | **Close** (where price ended) |
| Top of upper wick | **High** (highest price reached) |
| Bottom of lower wick | **Low** (lowest price reached) |

### Bearish Candlestick (Red by default)

| Part | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| Top of body | **Open** (where price started) |
| Bottom of body | **Close** (where price ended) |
| Top of upper wick | **High** (highest price reached) |
| Bottom of lower wick | **Low** (lowest price reached) |

Each new candlestick opens where the previous one closed. The color of candlesticks is customizable in chart settings.

## Time Frames & Multi-Timeframe Analysis

A **time frame** determines the interval each candlestick represents:

| Time Frame | Each Candlestick Represents |
|------------|----------------------------|
| 1M (1 minute) | 1 minute of price action |
| 5M (5 minutes) | 5 minutes of price action |
| 15M | 15 minutes of price action |
| 1H (1 hour) | 1 hour of price action |
| 4H | 4 hours of price action |
| D (Daily) | 1 full trading day |

**Multi-timeframe relationship:** A single 1-hour candlestick contains four 15-minute candlesticks, or sixty 1-minute candlesticks. Lower time frames provide more detail within the same price range.

> **Key Insight:** The lower the time frame, the more "noise" and false signals you'll see. Higher time frame levels (1H, 4H) are more reliable than lower time frame levels (1M, 5M).

## Candlestick Patterns

Candlestick patterns help predict whether price is likely to reverse or continue. No pattern is 100% reliable, but combining them with other strategies increases win rate.

### Bullish Engulfing

A green candlestick whose body is **larger than** the previous red candlestick. It "engulfs" (eats) the prior candle. This signals that buyers have overwhelmed sellers and price is likely to reverse upward.

### Bearish Engulfing

A red candlestick whose body is **larger than** the previous green candlestick. This signals that sellers have overwhelmed buyers and price is likely to reverse downward.

### Doji Candlestick

A candlestick with a **very small body** and **long wicks on both top and bottom** (preferably similar lengths). This indicates **indecision** — price tried to go up and down but ended roughly where it started. After a strong move up or down, a doji often signals an impending reversal.

> **Key Insight:** No candlestick pattern guarantees a reversal. Use them as **confluence** alongside support/resistance levels, market structure, and indicators for higher-probability setups.

## Market Structure

Understanding market structure is essential for determining which direction to trade.

### Bullish Market Structure (Uptrend)

Price creates **higher highs (HH)** and **higher lows (HL)**. Each peak is higher than the previous peak, and each pullback low is higher than the previous low.

### Bearish Market Structure (Downtrend)

Price creates **lower highs (LH)** and **lower lows (LL)**. Each peak is lower than the previous peak, and each trough is lower than the previous trough.

### Consolidating Market (Range)

Price moves within a defined range with no clear direction. Neither buyers nor sellers are in control. Price bounces between support and resistance without making clear higher highs or lower lows.

**Why this matters:** Certain strategies work better in trending markets (bullish or bearish), while others work better in consolidating markets. Identifying the current market condition tells you which strategies to deploy.

> **Key Insight:** Always **trade in the direction of the market**. Never try to predict tops or bottoms. Banks and hedge funds move the market — your job is to follow their direction, not fight it.

## Support & Resistance

Support and resistance are the **foundation of technical analysis** and the backbone of nearly every trading strategy.

### Definitions

- **Support** — a price level below current price where buyers historically stepped in, causing price to bounce up. Think of it as a **floor**.
- **Resistance** — a price level above current price where sellers historically stepped in, causing price to drop. Think of it as a **ceiling**.

### Why They Work

When an asset reaches a price that the market considers "too cheap" (support), institutional and retail buyers step in, driving price up. When it reaches a price considered "too expensive" (resistance), sellers take profits, driving price down. These reactions create repeatable zones.

### How to Draw Support & Resistance Zones

1. **Zoom out** on the 4-hour or 1-hour chart
2. Look for areas where price **rejected strongly** — a big move up from a low, or a big drop from a high
3. Draw a **box** (not a line) from:
   - **Support zone:** lowest wick → highest body of the candlestick before the bounce
   - **Resistance zone:** highest wick → lowest body of the candlestick before the drop
4. Drag the box across the chart to the right and wait for price to retest the zone

### Zone Strength

- Higher time frame zones (4H, 1H) are **stronger** than lower time frame zones (1M, 5M)
- Zones with **big, violent rejections** are stronger than zones with small reactions
- Start by identifying the **most obvious** zones before looking for subtle ones
- Avoid cluttering your chart with too many zones — focus on the clearest ones

> **Key Insight:** Price rarely stops at the *exact* same level twice. Using **boxes** instead of lines accounts for this variability and captures more retests.

## Break & Retest

When price **breaks through** a support or resistance zone, it typically comes back to **retest** that zone before continuing. The broken zone flips roles:

- A broken **support zone** becomes **resistance** on retest
- A broken **resistance zone** becomes **support** on retest

This is called a **break and retest** and is one of the highest-probability entry patterns. Wait for price to break a zone, then enter when it returns and confirms the flip.

## Indicators

Indicators are **sidekicks, not heroes**. They compile chart data into different visual formats but should never be relied on alone. Combine them with price action, support/resistance, and market structure.

> **Key Insight:** Do not clutter your chart with dozens of indicators. More indicators = more confusion, not more clarity.

### Exponential Moving Average (EMA)

The EMA shows the **average price** over a specified number of candlesticks. It helps determine trend direction:

- **Price above the EMA** → uptrend → look for buys
- **Price below the EMA** → downtrend → look for sells

**Common EMA settings:** 9, 30, 50, 100 periods. The 100 EMA is used in the HSS strategy.

### VWAP (Volume Weighted Average Price)

Similar to the EMA but weighted by volume:

- **Price below VWAP** → look for sells
- **Price above VWAP** → look for buys

### RSI (Relative Strength Index)

Displayed below the chart as an oscillator:

- **Price above the RSI upper band** → overbought → likely to reverse down
- **Price below the RSI lower band** → oversold → likely to reverse up

Best used as additional confluence, not as a standalone signal.

### Volume

Shows the number of buyers vs. sellers at each candlestick:

- **Large green bars** = strong buying volume (aligned with bullish candlesticks)
- **Large red bars** = strong selling volume (aligned with bearish candlesticks)

### Kill Zones Indicator

Displays trading session boundaries directly on the chart:

- **Yellow line** = London session open
- **White line** = New York session open
- Helps identify optimal trading windows visually

## Strategy 1: Hybrid Super Scalping (HSS)

The HSS is a **1-minute scalping strategy** using Heikin-Ashi candlesticks and the 100 EMA. It catches retracements (pullbacks) within a trending market.

### 6-Step Checklist

**Step 1 — Chart Setup:**
- Switch from regular candlesticks to **Heikin-Ashi** candlesticks
- Add the **100 EMA** indicator (length = 100)

**Step 2 — Market Structure:**
- **Buys only** when price is **above** the 100 EMA
- **Sells only** when price is **below** the 100 EMA
- Wait for market structure to form (higher lows for buys, lower highs for sells)

**Step 3 — Clean Pullback:**
- Wait for at least **2 consecutive candlesticks** moving against the trend
- **Clean** means no wicks on the side facing the trend direction:
  - For buys: 2+ red candlesticks with **no upper wicks**
  - For sells: 2+ green candlesticks with **no lower wicks**

**Step 4 — Entry (High-Volume Doji):**
- After the pullback, wait for a **doji candlestick** (small body, wicks top and bottom)
- **High-volume** = the doji must be **larger than at least 1 of the last 3 candlesticks**
- Enter on the close of the doji

**Step 5 — Stop Loss:**
- **Buys:** Place stop loss below the low of the doji wick
- **Sells:** Place stop loss above the high of the doji wick

**Step 6 — Take Profit:**
- Target a minimum **1:1 risk-to-reward ratio**
- Optional: hold for 2:1 R:R (win rate will drop from ~80% to ~70%)

**Optimal trading window:** 9:00 AM – 12:00 PM EST (highest volume period)

### Heikin-Ashi Candlesticks Explained

Heikin-Ashi candlesticks smooth price action by averaging:

- **Wicks** work the same as regular candlesticks
- **Bodies** show the *average* price, not the exact open/close
- The top/bottom of a body starts at the **midpoint** of the previous candlestick's body
- Makes trends and pullbacks easier to visualize

> **Key Insight:** Follow this checklist exactly. Do not deviate. If any step is not met, skip the trade.

## Strategy 2: London S&R Zone Strategy

This **intraday strategy** combines session analysis with support/resistance. It uses the **hourly time frame** for zone identification and the **1-minute time frame** for entries.

### Setup

- Add the **Kill Zones** indicator to your chart
- Focus on **London session** (yellow line) and **New York session** (white line)
- Time frame: **1H** for zones, **1M** for entries

### Step-by-Step

1. **Identify London session range** — mark the high and low of price action between the yellow line (London open) and white line (New York open)
2. **Draw support zone** at the London low (lowest wick → highest body)
3. **Draw resistance zone** at the London high (highest wick → lowest body)
4. **Wait for New York session** to begin
5. **Watch for price to tap into** either the London high or London low zone
6. **Go to the 1-minute time frame** for entry confirmation:
   - **For buys off London low:** Wait for price to break above the most recent swing high after tapping support
   - **For sells off London high:** Wait for price to break below the most recent swing low after tapping resistance
7. **Stop loss:** Place below the most recent swing low (for buys) or above the most recent swing high (for sells)
8. **Take profit:** Target recent swing highs (for buys) or recent swing lows (for sells)

### Break & Retest Variation

If price **breaks through** a London zone and then **retests** it, the flipped zone becomes an entry opportunity. A broken support becomes resistance (sell), and a broken resistance becomes support (buy).

> **Key Insight:** This strategy is simple because you're only marking two levels per day (London high and low) and waiting for price to come to you. No guessing required.

## Strategy 3: Squeeze (Triangle Breakout) Strategy

The squeeze strategy identifies **converging trend lines** (triangles) forming from lower highs and higher lows simultaneously. When price breaks out of this compression, it typically moves explosively.

### Setup

- **Time frames:** 15M (for identifying the squeeze), 5M (for entry)
- Can use regular or Heikin-Ashi candlesticks

### Step-by-Step

1. **Identify a squeeze** — look for at least **2 rejections at the upper trend line** and **2 rejections at the lower trend line** simultaneously
2. **Draw trend lines** connecting the highs and lows
3. **Wait for the squeeze to tighten** — the closer price gets to the apex, the more likely a breakout
4. **Go to the 5-minute time frame** when approaching an entry
5. **Enter on breakout confirmation:**
   - **Long entry:** Price breaks above the upper trend line **AND** breaks above the most recent swing high
   - **Short entry:** Price breaks below the lower trend line **AND** breaks below the most recent swing low
6. **Stop loss:** Below the most recent swing low (for longs) or above the most recent swing high (for shorts)
7. **Take profit:** Target recent swing highs/lows; minimum **2:1 risk-to-reward ratio**

> **Key Insight:** This strategy works across futures, crypto, and stocks — both intraday and swing trading. It is one of the most versatile strategies covered.

## Backtesting

Backtesting is **practice for traders** — equivalent to practice reps for athletes. It allows you to test strategies on historical data without risking real money, gaining hundreds of reps in a single session.

### How to Backtest on TradingView

1. Press the **Replay** button (bar icon in the top toolbar)
2. Click anywhere on the chart to set the starting point (ideally randomly, with eyes closed to avoid bias)
3. Set up your chart exactly as you would for live trading (same indicators, same time frames)
4. Use the **play**, **skip**, and **speed controls** to advance candlestick by candlestick
5. When you spot a valid setup, draw your entry, stop loss, and take profit using drawing tools
6. Record the result in your journal

### Best Practices

- **Trade the same instruments** you would trade live
- **Trade the same time window** you would trade live (e.g., 8 AM – 1 PM EST)
- **Use the same strategy** you plan to use live
- **Track every trade** — win/loss, ticks, R:R ratio, time, day, direction, reasoning
- Aim for **1–2 hours of backtesting per day** for rapid improvement
- FXReplay (fxreplay.com) is an alternative platform that offers extended historical data and automatic trade tracking

> **Key Insight:** You can take 20–100+ backtesting reps in a single session vs. 1–3 live trades per day. Backtesting accelerates chart reading ability and strategy mastery dramatically.

## Trading Journal

Journaling is essential for identifying patterns in your trading behavior, learning from mistakes, and refining strategies over time.

### What to Track

| Field | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Date & Time** | When the trade was taken |
| **Instrument** | What ticker was traded (NQ, ES, etc.) |
| **Direction** | Long (buy) or short (sell) |
| **Position Size** | Number of contracts |
| **Risk Amount** | Dollar amount at risk if stop loss is hit |
| **Expected Profit** | Dollar amount if take profit is hit |
| **Strategy Used** | Which strategy was applied |
| **Entry Reasoning** | Why you entered (e.g., "break of recent high after London retest") |
| **Outcome** | Win, loss, or break-even |
| **Actual P&L** | Dollar profit or loss |
| **Trade Management** | Did you move stop loss, take partials, or exit early? |
| **Market Conditions** | News events, volume level, choppiness |
| **Why You Won/Lost** | One-sentence self-assessment |

### Journal Benefits

- **Pattern recognition** — discover that you perform better on certain days, times, or with certain strategies
- **Emotional management** — zoom out from individual losses to see the bigger profitable picture
- **Accountability** — knowing you must record every trade discourages impulsive entries
- **Risk monitoring** — spot dangerous spikes in risk per trade before they become habits

### Automated Journaling

Platforms like **Wagmi Trader** can connect to your brokerage/prop firm account and automatically track most fields. You'll still need to manually add entry reasoning and win/loss analysis.

**Review schedule:**
- Weekly: Review the past week's trades (e.g., every Saturday)
- Monthly: Review the past month to identify longer-term patterns

## Prop Firms & Funded Accounts

Prop firms (proprietary trading firms) allow you to trade with **their capital** instead of your own. You pay a fee to take a challenge, prove you can trade, and then receive a funded account.

### How Prop Firms Work

1. **Pay a fee** (e.g., $500) to purchase a challenge account (e.g., $100,000)
2. **Pass the challenge** by hitting a profit target without exceeding the max loss limit
3. **Receive a funded account** — a fresh account you can trade for real profits
4. **Keep 80–90%** of profits generated on the funded account
5. If you fail the challenge, you lose only the initial fee — **no additional debt**

### Prop Firm Risk Management Guidelines

| Account Size | Max Risk Per Trade |
|--------------|-------------------|
| $25,000 | $125 |
| $50,000 | $250 |
| $100,000 | $500 |
| $150,000 | $750 |

*These limits correspond to approximately 0.5% of account size per trade.*

### Math Example

On a $100K account risking $500/trade with a 2:1 R:R and 50% win rate over 20 trades:
- 10 losses × $500 = **-$5,000**
- 10 wins × $1,000 = **+$10,000**
- **Net profit: $5,000** from a ~$500 investment

Using **multiple linked accounts** (e.g., 5 × $100K), the same 20 trades yield **$25,000** with no additional work.

### Prop Firm Tips

- **Don't trade every day** — only take A+ setups on funded accounts
- **Don't over-leverage** — stick to the risk limits in the table above
- **Create a separate trading plan** for your funded account with stricter rules
- **Use payouts to fund a personal live account** — this is the recommended progression

> **Key Insight:** Prop firms are the recommended starting path for traders without large capital. Use prop firm payouts to build your own live account over time.

## Building Your Trading Plan

A trading plan is a **self-imposed rulebook** that forces consistency and removes emotion from decision-making. In a profession with no boss, the trading plan is your accountability system.

### What to Include

1. **Trader type** — scalper, intraday, or swing trader
2. **Trading window** — specific hours when you're allowed to trade (e.g., 8 AM – 12 PM EST)
3. **Instruments** — maximum 1–2 tickers to focus on
4. **Strategy** — maximum 1–2 strategies to use
5. **Max risk per trade** — dollar amount or percentage of account
6. **Max risk per day** — total dollar loss limit before stopping
7. **Max trades per day** — typically 2–3 maximum
8. **Entry criteria** — specific, non-negotiable conditions for entering a trade
9. **Exit criteria** — when to take profit, when to exit early, when to hold longer
10. **News rules** — e.g., no trading 5 minutes before/after red-folder news
11. **Mental state rules** — conditions under which you should not trade (emotional distress, fatigue, etc.)
12. **Consequences for breaking rules** — the most important element

### Consequences

Losing money is **not** a strong enough consequence to prevent repeated mistakes. Choose a physical consequence you genuinely dislike:

- Ice bath or cold shower
- Running 3 miles
- Some other uncomfortable physical task

The consequence must be:
- **Immediate** — executed as soon as the rule is broken
- **Unpleasant** — enough to create a visceral deterrent
- **Consistent** — applied every single time, no exceptions

> **Key Insight:** Read your trading plan every morning and before every trade. Post it physically next to your screen. The consequence system is the single most effective tool for building discipline.

## Trading Psychology

Day trading is **80% psychology**. The strategies work — the difference between profitable and unprofitable traders is mental discipline, not strategy quality.

### Core Psychological Principles

**Think in probabilities:**
- Each individual trade is like a coin flip — even a 70% win rate strategy will lose 3 out of 10 trades
- Probabilities only reveal themselves over **large sample sizes** (100+ trades)
- Don't judge a strategy by the last 5 trades; judge it over the last 100

**Success = following your plan, not winning:**
- A day where you followed every rule but lost money is a **good day**
- A day where you broke rules but made money is a **bad day**
- You cannot control individual trade outcomes — you can only control whether you gave yourself the best chance

**Develop a carefree (not careless) mindset:**
- Fear of losing clouds judgment and causes late entries or skipped setups
- Losing is a **business expense** in trading, not a personal failure
- Every trader loses, including professionals with 8+ years of experience

### Actionable Psychology Tips

1. **Read your trading plan** every morning and before every trade
2. **Create a pre-trade plan** for each trade before entering — outline entry, stop, target, and management rules while you're still thinking logically
3. **Imagine your stop loss is money already spent** — once you enter, assume that money is gone; this prevents moving your stop loss
4. **Switch to a higher time frame** after entering a trade to avoid reacting to noise
5. **Walk away from your screen** after entering — use TradingView alerts for key levels
6. **Switch to Heikin-Ashi** or change all candlesticks to one color after entering to reduce emotional reactions to individual candles
7. **If your heart races** when entering a trade, close it immediately — this signals over-leveraging or lack of confidence in the setup
8. **Win or lose, if you followed your rules, celebrate** — the process matters more than the outcome

### If You're Profitable on Demo, You Already Know How to Trade

The only difference between demo success and live results is psychology. On demo, there's no fear, no greed, no emotional attachment to the money. The goal is to replicate that mental state with real capital by internalizing these psychological principles.
